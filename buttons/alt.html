Alfred Lord Tennyson

1809 — 1892

"'Tis better to have loved and lost / Than never to have loved at all."

I

Life & Biography

Six facts that define Tennyson — know these cold
  • Poet Laureate for 42 years — 1850 to 1892; the longest laureate tenure in history; no other poet has held the office so long or been so identified with a nation's literary identity
  • "In Memoriam" took 17 years to write — Arthur Hallam died in 1833; the poem was published in 1850; the grief was not performance, it was the shaping experience of his entire creative life
  • Darwin's Origin of Species appeared the same year as Idylls of the King began — Tennyson was the poet of Victorian evolutionary anxiety a decade before Darwin; "In Memoriam" anticipates "nature red in tooth and claw" and the crisis of faith
  • Victoria read "In Memoriam" after Albert died — she said it comforted her more than almost anything except the Bible; the Queen and the Laureate were bound by public grief in a way no poet-monarch relationship had been before
  • The most quoted poet in English after Shakespeare — "Tis better to have loved and lost," "Theirs not to reason why," "To strive, to seek, to find, and not to yield" — all Tennyson; most people who quote him don't know they're doing it
  • Turned down a baronetcy four times before accepting — became Baron Tennyson of Aldworth in 1883; the first poet to be elevated to the peerage purely for literary achievement
Origins — a clergyman's son in Lincolnshire
Born 6 August 1809 at Somersby, Lincolnshire — fourth of twelve children of the Revd George Clayton Tennyson. The family was deeply literary and deeply dysfunctional: his father was disinherited in favour of a younger brother, drove himself to alcoholism, and died in 1831 a broken man. Three of Tennyson's brothers suffered serious mental illness. The strain of hereditary madness haunted Tennyson throughout his life — he called it "the black blood of the Tennysons" — and directly shaped his poetry's recurrent obsession with instability, dissolution, and the precariousness of mental order.
Cambridge — the Apostles & Arthur Hallam
Entered Trinity College, Cambridge, in 1827. Won the Chancellor's Gold Medal for poetry in 1829 with "Timbuctoo." Joined the Cambridge Apostles — an elite intellectual discussion society whose members included Richard Chenevix Trench, Richard Monckton Milnes, James Spedding, and, crucially, Arthur Henry Hallam. Hallam — brilliant, socially gifted, deeply kind — became Tennyson's closest friend and was engaged to Tennyson's sister Emily. His sudden death from a brain haemorrhage in Vienna on 15 September 1833, aged 22, was the defining catastrophe of Tennyson's emotional and creative life. Everything changed.
The ten silent years (1832–1842)
After the hostile reviews of his 1832 Poems (Croker in the Quarterly Review called "The Lady of Shalott" nonsense and "O Darling Room" an embarrassment), Tennyson published nothing for ten years — 1832 to 1842. He worked in private, revised obsessively, and grieved. When Poems (1842) finally appeared — in two volumes, the first containing revised earlier work, the second containing new poems — it was an immediate critical success. "Morte d'Arthur," "Locksley Hall," "Ulysses," and "Break, Break, Break" were all in the 1842 volume. The ten-year silence was one of the most productive periods of revision in English literary history.
1850 — the great year
Three things happened in 1850 that transformed Tennyson's life. In May, In Memoriam A.H.H. was published — anonymously, though the authorship was quickly known. It was an immediate triumph: six editions in the first year. In June, he married Emily Sellwood (to whom he had been engaged, on and off, for fourteen years — the engagement was broken in 1840 due to his financial instability and religious doubt). In November, Wordsworth died; Tennyson was appointed Poet Laureate. He was 41, finally solvent (In Memoriam's sales), finally married, and suddenly the nation's official poet. The next 42 years were outwardly stable, inwardly anxious.
Laureate years — Maud, Idylls, the public poet
As Laureate, Tennyson wrote "Ode on the Death of the Duke of Wellington" (1852), "The Charge of the Light Brigade" (1854 — within weeks of the battle), "Maud" (1855 — his most experimental and most controversial poem), and began Idylls of the King (first instalment 1859). He retreated from London to Farringford on the Isle of Wight and later to Aldworth in Surrey — the Victorian equivalent of going offline. Day-trippers came to spy on him; he wore a wide-brimmed hat and a cloak as a disguise. The tension between the public role and the private artist defines the laureate years.
Death & legacy
Died 6 October 1892, aged 83 — a volume of Shakespeare open on his bed, moonlight through the window, family around him. His last completed poem was "Crossing the Bar" (1889) — which he requested always be printed last in any edition of his works. Buried in Westminster Abbey, in Poets' Corner. His funeral brought thousands into the streets. He had been Britain's national poet for four decades and the best-selling poet in the English-speaking world for most of them. The Victorian age's most representative literary voice — and the first to be dismantled by the Modernists who came after.

II

In Memoriam A.H.H.

In Memoriam — eight things to know immediately
  • 131 sections + Prologue + Epilogue — not 131 poems but one continuous poem in sections; the largest sustained elegy in English
  • The "In Memoriam stanza" — Tennyson's own invention: four lines of iambic tetrameter, rhyming ABBA; the enclosed rhyme enacts enclosure, circling, return to grief
  • Published anonymously — 1850; quickly known; Queen Victoria said it comforted her after Albert's death in 1861 more than anything except the Bible
  • Not straightforward grief — the poem asks: can we believe in personal immortality after Darwin? Can love survive death? Can faith survive science? These questions make it a Victorian crisis-of-faith document, not merely an elegy
  • Three Christmases structure the poem — the elegiac movement marked by three Christmases after Hallam's death; the poem tracks grief's evolution over years
  • "Nature, red in tooth and claw" — Section LVI; written before Darwin's Origin (1859); Tennyson anticipates evolutionary theory's assault on providential nature
  • T.S. Eliot called it "the greatest poem of the 19th century" — while also calling Tennyson a poet of "impeccable technique and mediocre thinking"; the ambivalence is itself a critical fact
  • The "Prologue" was written last — after the body of the poem; it is addressed to "Strong Son of God, immortal Love" — faith reached at the end of doubt, not before it

▼
The In Memoriam stanza
I held it truth, with him who sings To one clear harp in divers tones, That men may rise on stepping-stones Of their dead selves to higher things.
Why ABBA and not ABAB
The enclosed rhyme scheme (ABBA) is the formal enactment of grief. An ABAB rhyme scheme moves forward — each B rhyme leads to the next stanza. ABBA turns back on itself — the poem circles, returns, encloses. The opening rhyme word is surrounded by the second pair before the stanza can close. This is what grief does: it keeps returning to the same point. Tennyson did not invent the stanza — it appears in Ben Jonson and Sidney — but his sustained use of it for 131 sections made it his own. Called the "In Memoriam stanza" in all subsequent literary reference.
The poem's movement — three Christmases
Sections I–XXVII: First grief — raw, questioning, desperate. The ship bringing Hallam's body home. The old house at Somersby. Dark house. Visiting the house where Hallam lived.
Sections XXVIII–XLIV: First Christmas — the family's first Christmas after the death; grief cannot celebrate.
Sections XLV–LXXVII: The middle period — philosophical wrestling; the nature red in tooth and claw passage; questions about the soul, immortality, evolution.
Sections LXXVIII–CIV: Second Christmas — grief softening, some acceptance.
Sections CV–CXXXI: Third Christmas and beyond — resolution; the move from Somersby; a vision of Hallam; the Epilogue (a marriage song) asserting life continues.
The movement is not from grief to joy but from paralysing grief to a provisional, hard-won acceptance. It is not a triumphant poem.
Key sections — learn these for NET
Section I ("I held it truth"): Opening statement — the poem's first uncertainty about received wisdom.
Section II ("Old yew, which graspest at the stones"): The yew tree as emblem of death and geological time — "Of all this mounded earth below."
Section V ("I sometimes hold it half a sin"): The inadequacy of language for grief — "In words, like weeds, I'll wrap me o'er."
Section VII ("Dark house, by which once more I stand"): Tennyson stands before Hallam's London house — the "blank day" of empty grief; one of the most perfect lyric moments in the poem.
Section XI ("Calm is the morn without a sound"): Calm morning that the speaker cannot feel — pathetic fallacy reversed.
Section LIV–LVI: The faith-and-doubt crisis — "We have but faith: we cannot know"; "Are God and Nature then at strife?" culminating in "Nature, red in tooth and claw."
Section XCV: The mystical vision — Tennyson reads Hallam's letters by night and experiences a trance in which he feels Hallam's presence. The closest the poem comes to direct communication with the dead.
Section CXXX ("Thy voice is on the rolling air"): Hallam present everywhere in nature — Wordsworthian consolation reached late.
Section CXXXI ("O living will that shalt endure"): Final section, addressed to "the will that stirs in mortal frames" — a post-Christian hope.
The "nature, red in tooth and claw" passage
Are God and Nature then at strife, That Nature lends such evil dreams? So careful of the type she seems, So careless of the single life; That I, considering everywhere Her secret meaning in her deeds, And finding that of fifty seeds She often brings but one to bear, I falter where I firmly trod, And falling with my weight of cares Upon the great world's altar-stairs That slope thro' darkness up to God, I stretch lame hands of faith, and grope, And gather dust and chaff, and call To what I feel is Lord of all, And faintly trust the larger hope.
The "dark house" section
Dark house, by which once more I stand Here in the long unlovely street, Doors, where my heart was used to beat So quickly, waiting for a hand, A hand that can be clasped no more — Behold me, for I cannot sleep, And like a guilty thing I creep At earliest morning to the door. He is not here; but far away The noise of life begins again, And ghastly thro' the drizzling rain On the bald street breaks the blank day.
Critical readings
T.S. Eliot: In his 1936 essay "In Memoriam" — called it "the greatest poem of the nineteenth century" but also "the concentrated diary of a man confessing himself" rather than a work of dramatic imagination. Identified the "range of feeling" as unequalled, the philosophical content as thin.
A.C. Bradley: Read the poem as a genuine philosophical progress — not just emotional record but intellectual journey from doubt to provisional faith.
John D. Jump: The poem's significance lies in its honest acknowledgement that faith cannot be philosophically demonstrated — it can only be trusted, "faintly."
Alan Sinfield (New Historicist): In Memoriam as a document of Victorian masculine friendship — the intensity of feeling between men that Victorian culture simultaneously endorsed (as manly friendship) and could not fully acknowledge.
Christopher Ricks: The most precise close reader of Tennyson — demonstrated how the poem's smallest verbal choices (prepositions, tenses, sound patterns) enact its emotional movements.
III

Idylls of the King

Idylls of the King — five things to get right
  • 12 idylls published over 26 years — first instalment 1859 ("Enid," "Vivien," "Elaine," "Guinevere"); final complete edition with "Balin and Balan" 1885; each instalment was a literary event
  • Not a retelling — an allegory — Arthur = ideal of spiritual and social order; the Round Table = Victorian civilisation; its dissolution = the crisis of Victorian values under materialism and sensuality
  • Merlin and Vivien is the key allegorical section — Vivien seduces and destroys Merlin (wisdom and spiritual power) through sensuality; the allegory is about the vulnerability of the mind to the body's claims
  • Guinevere is Tennyson's most Victorian section — Arthur's speech to the fallen Guinevere is sanctimonious even by Victorian standards; modern readers find it morally repellent; it was enormously popular in 1859
  • The model is not Malory alone — Tennyson used Malory's Le Morte d'Arthur, the Mabinogion (Welsh sources), and Geoffrey of Monmouth; he departed significantly from all of them

The twelve idylls in order
The Coming of Arthur — Arthur's mysterious birth; Guinevere's doubt about his legitimacy; the founding of the ideal order.
Gareth and Lynette — a young knight proves himself; comic-romance tone; the most hopeful idyll.
The Marriage of Geraint / Geraint and Enid — jealousy, faith, patient endurance; Enid as the ideal of feminine loyalty.
Balin and Balan — added last (1885); two brothers; the dangers of the violent temperament uncontrolled by higher values.
Merlin and Vivien — the central allegory: Vivien (sensuality, deception, the world's cunning) destroys Merlin (wisdom, spiritual authority). The moment Arthur's order begins to crumble from within.
Lancelot and Elaine — the "lily maid of Astolat" loves Lancelot; dies unrequited; Lancelot is trapped between his love for Guinevere and his honour. The pathos of destroyed innocence.
The Holy Grail — the quest that destroys the Round Table; Galahad achieves it; Percivale and others fail; Arthur laments that the quest took his best knights from the real work of building a just kingdom.
Pelleas and Ettarre — innocence betrayed; a young knight destroyed by an indifferent woman; the Round Table's corruption spreading.
The Last Tournament — the tournament of the dead innocence; everything is contaminated; even Tristram, once noble, is cynical.
Guinevere — Guinevere in the nunnery; Arthur's long speech of reproach and forgiveness; the most controversially sentimental section.
The Passing of Arthur — based on the 1842 "Morte d'Arthur"; the wounded king taken to Avalon; Bedivere throws Excalibur; the order dissolves. "The old order changeth, yielding place to new."
To the Queen — the Epilogue; Tennyson speaks directly as the poet and Victorian citizen.
The allegorical framework
Tennyson himself stated the allegory in a note: Arthur represents "ideal manhood clothed with real man." The three queens who take Arthur to Avalon represent Faith, Hope, and Charity. Guinevere represents the flesh; Lancelot the highest human type that is not spiritual enough; the Grail represents spiritual aspiration that, taken too absolutely, destroys the social fabric. The allegory is Victorian anxieties dressed in medieval clothing: the corruption of the ideal by sensuality, the failure of human institutions, the impossibility of sustaining the spiritual in the material world.
The closing lines — "The Passing of Arthur"
The old order changeth, yielding place to new, And God fulfils himself in many ways, Lest one good custom should corrupt the world. Comfort thyself: what comfort is in me? I have lived my life, and that which I have done May He within himself make pure! but thou, If thou shouldst never see my face again, Pray for my soul. More things are wrought by prayer Than this world dreams of.
Critical debate — allegory vs narrative
The dominant Victorian criticism praised the moral allegory. 20th-century critics (notably Buckley, Rosenberg) found the allegory too heavy — that it distorts the Arthurian material, making characters into Victorian types rather than mythological figures. The Guinevere idyll in particular has attracted feminist criticism: Arthur's lengthy speech of forgiveness-as-condescension exemplifies Victorian sexual double standards — his own failures (as a distant, idealising, possibly asexual husband) are invisible to the poem's moral framework. John D. Rosenberg's The Fall of Camelot (1973) is the essential modern study.
IV

Major Lyric Poems

Ulysses NET HIGH
Written October 1833 · Published 1842 · Dramatic monologue · 70 lines
▼
Ulysses — five points to know
  • Written days after Hallam's death — Tennyson said it expressed "my feeling about the need of going forward, and braving the struggle of life" — grief processed through Ulysses's defiance
  • Not Homer's Odysseus but Dante's Ulisse — Dante's Ulysses (Inferno XXVI) sets out on a final fatal voyage beyond the known world; Tennyson's Ulysses is that figure
  • The dramatic monologue form — Ulysses speaks; we hear only him; we must judge his speech; is he heroic or irresponsible? This ironic distance is not fully Victorian — it anticipates Browning
  • "To strive, to seek, to find, and not to yield" — the final line; inscribed on the Scott memorial in Antarctica; used in the 2012 London Olympics closing ceremony; the most quoted Tennyson line after "better to have loved and lost"
  • The middle section on Telemachus — Ulysses praises his son Telemachus in terms that are also slightly condescending; Telemachus will do the "useful" civic work while Ulysses does the adventurous; the contrast reveals Ulysses's self-knowledge and self-deception simultaneously
The poem
It little profits that an idle king, By this still hearth, among these barren crags, Matched with an aged wife, I mete and dole Unequal laws unto a savage race, That hoard, and sleep, and feed, and know not me. I cannot rest from travel: I will drink Life to the lees...
I am a part of all that I have met; Yet all experience is an arch wherethro' Gleams that untravell'd world whose margin fades For ever and for ever when I move. How dull it is to pause, to make an end, To rust unburnished, not to shine in use!
Though much is taken, much abides; and though We are not now that strength which in old days Moved earth and heaven, that which we are, we are — One equal temper of heroic hearts, Made weak by time and fate, but strong in will To strive, to seek, to find, and not to yield.
Critical debate — heroism or escapism?
The key critical debate about Ulysses: is he heroic or irresponsible? Two readings:

The heroic reading (traditional): Ulysses represents the indomitable human spirit — refusal to accept limitations, the drive to experience, the dignity of continued striving even in old age. The poem gives voice to human aspiration at its noblest.

The ironic reading (E.J. Chell, W.W. Robson): Ulysses is a man abandoning his duties — his kingdom, his wife, his son — for selfish adventure; he rationalises this with magnificent rhetoric; his praise of Telemachus is condescending; the heroic language covers a failure of responsibility. If we apply the dramatic monologue's ironic distance, Ulysses becomes a study in self-delusion.

Tennyson himself endorsed the heroic reading; most NET-era questions expect the heroic reading as primary while acknowledging the ironic dimension.
The Lady of Shalott NET HIGH
1832 (revised 1842) · 4 parts, 19 stanzas · Nine-line stanza with refrain
▼
Lady of Shalott — four points to know
  • Art vs life — the Lady weaves what she sees only in a mirror; when she turns to see Lancelot directly, the curse falls; the poem is about the artist who must mediate reality through representation, and what happens when she refuses
  • The Pre-Raphaelite poem — Holman Hunt, Waterhouse, and others painted it obsessively; it became the defining Pre-Raphaelite subject; the Lady is their icon of doomed female beauty
  • Gender and enclosure — feminist readings: the Lady is confined by a male-imposed curse to aesthetic production for male consumption; her "crime" is desiring to look directly at the world rather than its mirror-image
  • The refrain changes — "The Lady of Shalott" is repeated at the end of every stanza, but its meaning shifts: from label to identity to death notice; the repetition is the formal enactment of the curse's inescapability
The turning point
His broad clear brow in sunlight glow'd; On burnish'd hooves his war-horse trode; From underneath his helmet flow'd His coal-black curls as on he rode, As he rode down to Camelot. From the bank and from the river He flash'd into the crystal mirror, "Tirra lirra," by the river Sang Sir Lancelot. She left the web, she left the loom, She made three paces thro' the room, She saw the water-lily bloom, She saw the helmet and the plume, She look'd down to Camelot. Out flew the web and floated wide; The mirror crack'd from side to side; "The curse is come upon me," cried The Lady of Shalott.
The Lotos-Eaters NET HIGH
1832 (revised 1842) · Spenserian stanzas + Choric Song
▼
Argument & structure
Based on Homer's Odyssey (Book IX): Odysseus's sailors eat the lotus fruit and lose all desire to return home. Tennyson develops this into a sustained meditation on the appeal of passive withdrawal from duty, struggle, and the demands of the world. The poem opens in Spenserian stanzas — meditative, sensuous, slowing — then shifts into the "Choric Song" in which the sailors systematically argue for staying: Why should they struggle? Nature rests; let them rest. The gods themselves are indifferent; why should mortals toil?
The Choric Song — the temptation of indolence
There is sweet music here that softer falls Than petals from blown roses on the grass, Or night-dews on still waters between walls Of shadowy granite, in a gleaming pass; Music that gentlier on the spirit lies, Than tired eyelids upon tired eyes; Music that brings sweet sleep down from the blissful skies. Here are cool mosses deep, And through the moss the ivies creep, And in the stream the long-leaved flowers weep, And from the craggy ledge the poppy hangs in sleep.
Critical significance
The poem is Tennyson's most sustained exploration of temptation — specifically, the temptation to abandon duty for sensation. This makes it both a Victorian moral poem (duty must triumph over ease) and an aestheticist poem (the ease is described with such beauty that it is clearly attractive). The sailors' final argument — "Let us swear an oath and keep it with an equal mind, / In the hollow Lotos-land to live and lie reclined" — is not endorsed by the poem but is seductively presented.

Comparison with Keats's "Ode on Indolence" is inevitable and productive: both poems give beauty to what they nominally reject. But Tennyson's poem is more explicitly social — the sailors have duties, families, a home — while Keats's is a private aesthetic decision.
Tithonus
Composed 1833 · Revised & published 1860 · Dramatic monologue · Blank verse
▼
Argument & significance
Tithonus — granted immortality by Aurora but not eternal youth — speaks his longing to die. The mythological situation gives Tennyson a vehicle for exploring what he could not say directly after Hallam's death: the desire to accept death, to complete the cycle, to release the self from the burden of continuing to exist without the beloved. Written in the same period as "Ulysses" — the two poems are mirror images. Where Ulysses defiantly refuses death, Tithonus longingly desires it. Both emerge from the same crisis of 1833.

The poem's central image — "The woods decay, the woods decay and fall, / The vapours weep their burthen to the sea, / Man comes and tills the field and lies beneath, / And after many a summer dies the swan" — is Tennyson's most beautiful statement of natural mutability as consolation rather than threat.
Break, Break, Break & Tears, Idle Tears
Two key shorter elegies
▼
Break, break, break, On thy cold gray stones, O Sea! And I would that my tongue could utter The thoughts that arise in me. O, well for the fisherman's boy, That he shouts with his sister at play! O, well for the sailor lad, That he sings in his boat on the bay! And the tender grace of a day that is dead Will never come back to me.
Break, Break, Break
Published 1842. The poem's power comes from its refusal to articulate grief directly — the speaker "would that my tongue could utter / The thoughts that arise in me" but cannot. The sea breaks endlessly; children play; sailors sing; the world continues indifferent to private grief. The contrast between the world's continuation and the speaker's loss is the entire emotional content. The final line — "the tender grace of a day that is dead / Will never come back to me" — is the plainest statement of irreversibility in Tennyson.
Tears, Idle Tears
Tears, idle tears, I know not what they mean, Tears from the depth of some divine despair Rise in the heart, and gather to the eyes, In looking on the happy autumn-fields, And thinking of the days that are no more.
From The Princess (1847) — a lyric embedded in a larger narrative poem. Sung by one of the women students at the Princess's women's college. Formally remarkable: it is in blank verse but its repetitions ("days that are no more" appears four times) create an almost incantatory effect. The grief is for an unnamed, unspecified past — not for a specific person, which makes it universally applicable. Keats called this effect "the pang of loss without its specific object" — Tennyson achieves it here more purely than anywhere else in his work.
The Charge of the Light Brigade Laureate poem
Published The Examiner, 9 December 1854 · Dactylic rhythm
▼
Charge of the Light Brigade — key facts
  • Written in minutes — inspired by a Times dispatch; Tennyson said he wrote the first draft in a few minutes; the published version was lightly revised
  • "Theirs not to reason why, / Theirs but to do and die" — the most quoted lines; deeply ambiguous: is this heroic nobility or an indictment of military incompetence that sent men to die for a misunderstood order?
  • The dactylic metre mimics hoofbeats — "Half a league, half a league, half a league onward" — the rhythm enacts the cavalry charge; Tennyson's most successful use of onomatopoeia at the structural level
  • Wilfred Owen and the war poets implicitly rejected it — Dulce et Decorum Est is partly a rebuttal of the Tennysonian glorification of military sacrifice; WWI poetry is defined partly by its rejection of this tradition
Half a league, half a league, Half a league onward, All in the valley of Death Rode the six hundred. "Forward, the Light Brigade! Charge for the guns!" he said. Into the valley of Death Rode the six hundred.
Crossing the Bar
1889 · Last poem · Written on the Solent ferry in twenty minutes
▼
Sunset and evening star, And one clear call for me! And may there be no moaning of the bar, When I put out to sea, But such a tide as moving seems asleep, Too full for sound and foam, When that which drew from out the boundless deep Turns again home. Twilight and evening bell, And after that the dark! And may there be no sadness of farewell, When I embark; For though from out our bourne of Time and Place The flood may bear me far, I hope to see my Pilot face to face When I have crossed the bar.
Significance
Written in 1889, three years before his death, on a crossing of the Solent — Tennyson said the poem came to him in twenty minutes. He asked that it always be placed last in any collection of his poems — his own editorial instruction, not a publisher's decision. The "bar" is the sandbar at the mouth of a harbour — the moment a ship passes from sheltered water to open sea; metaphorically, the threshold of death. The poem's Christian belief is expressed quietly — the "Pilot" is Christ — without the anguished wrestling of In Memoriam. It represents the faith arrived at after decades of doubt: not triumphant, but calm.
V

Dramatic Monologues & Narratives

Tennyson and the dramatic monologue
  • He pioneered it; Browning perfected it — "Ulysses," "Tithonus," "St. Simeon Stylites," "Northern Farmer" are dramatic monologues; Tennyson got there before or simultaneously with Browning's Dramatic Lyrics (1842)
  • His monologues are less ironic than Browning's — Browning's speakers are consistently unreliable; Tennyson's speakers (Ulysses, Tithonus) are partially reliable; the gap between speaker and poet is smaller
  • "St. Simeon Stylites" is his most satirical — the ascetic saint on his pillar is clearly self-deceived; the poem is Tennyson's most Browning-like in its ironic distance
Maud — A Monodrama Controversial
1855 · Sequence of lyrics forming a dramatic narrative · Multiple metres
▼
What Maud is
Tennyson called Maud a "monodrama" — a single speaker's psychological narrative told through lyrics in different metres, each metre matching the speaker's emotional state. The speaker (unnamed, unstable, probably neurasthenic) loves Maud, kills her brother in a duel, goes mad, recovers his sanity by volunteering for the Crimean War. Tennyson considered it his masterpiece and read it aloud to visitors at every opportunity. Critics and the public were divided: they found the speaker's psychology morbidly convincing but the pro-war ending morally repellent.
Key passage — "Come into the garden, Maud"
Come into the garden, Maud, For the black bat, night, has flown, Come into the garden, Maud, I am here at the gate alone; And the woodbine spices are wafted abroad, And the musk of the rose is blown.
Critical significance
The form: Multiple metres matching emotional states is technically revolutionary — Tennyson uses anapaests for excitement, slow trochees for depression, iambic pentameter for rational reflection. The form makes the speaker's psychology legible through sound.
The war ending: The speaker "heals" by embracing the Crimean War as a moral cause — "It is better to fight for the good than to rail at the ill." Gladstone attacked Tennyson for militarism; later critics found the ending psychologically dishonest — the speaker's "recovery" looks like a different form of madness.
The psychological portrait: The speaker is Tennyson's most complex psychological creation — a man on the edge of sanity whose perceptions are unreliable throughout. The poem anticipates the interior monologue of Modernism.
Locksley Hall
1842 · Trochaic octameter · Dramatic monologue
▼
Significance
A young man returns to Locksley Hall, where he loved his cousin Amy, who abandoned him for a wealthier match. The poem moves through rage, contempt, and the consolation of imagining the future — including a vision of aerial warfare ("the nations' airy navies grappling in the central blue") and the Parliament of man ("the Federation of the world"). The poem contains Tennyson's most explicit statement of Victorian progressivism: "Yet I doubt not through the ages one increasing purpose runs, / And the thoughts of men are widened with the process of the suns."

Tennyson returned to the subject in Locksley Hall Sixty Years After (1886) — the same speaker, now old, finding the earlier optimism naive. The disillusioned sequel is more honest but less memorable.
VI

Early Poems & Key Shorter Works

The Palace of Art
1832 (revised 1842) · Quatrains · Allegory
▼
Argument
A Soul builds a Palace of Art — a perfect aesthetic retreat stocked with the world's beauty, philosophy, and art — and withdraws there from human community. After three years the Soul is seized with a horror of isolation and guilt: "I am void, / Dark, formless, utterly destroyed." She leaves the Palace for "a cottage in the vale" — resolving to return only when she has served her time of social engagement. The poem is Tennyson's most direct statement of the Victorian anxiety about aestheticism: beauty without social function becomes spiritual death. The poem anticipates Wilde's critique of aestheticism from within the aesthetic tradition — though Tennyson resolves it conservatively (return to society) where Wilde would not.
Mariana
1830 · Seven stanzas · Refrain poem
▼
Significance
Based on a character in Shakespeare's Measure for Measure — Mariana waiting at the moated grange for Angelo who will never come. Tennyson removes all narrative context: we have only the woman, the decaying house, the landscape of stagnation, and the refrain: "He cometh not," she said; / "I am aweary, aweary, / I would that I were dead!" The poem is pure atmosphere — the pathetic fallacy at its most sustained, the external world completely matching and reinforcing the psychological state. The poem has attracted feminist readings: Mariana is defined entirely by absence, by waiting for the man who defines her existence. She has no identity outside this waiting.
Morte d'Arthur
1842 · Blank verse · Later incorporated into Idylls
▼
Significance
The 1842 "Morte d'Arthur" is a self-contained poem — Arthur's death, Bedivere's reluctance to throw Excalibur, the barge to Avalon. Tennyson later revised and incorporated it as "The Passing of Arthur" in the Idylls (1869). The 1842 version is embedded in a frame — a Christmas Eve reading among friends — that Tennyson added to legitimise his use of Arthurian material. The blank verse is among Tennyson's finest: "So all day long the noise of battle roll'd / Among the mountains by the winter sea." For NET: the 1842 poem and the 1869 Idylls version differ — the Idylls version adds a prelude and conclusion.
VII

Major Themes

Tennyson's five great subjects
  • Faith vs doubt — the central Victorian crisis; In Memoriam is the definitive literary document; Tennyson's faith was always provisional, always earned through wrestling, never easy
  • Time and loss — everything passes; the past is irreversible; the dead do not return; the only question is how to continue; "Tears, Idle Tears," "Break, Break, Break," In Memoriam, Tithonus all circle this
  • Duty vs desire — the demand to serve society vs the pull of aesthetic withdrawal; Ulysses, The Lotos-Eaters, The Palace of Art, The Lady of Shalott all stage this conflict
  • The evolution of civilisation — Tennyson was the poet of Victorian progress and its costs; Idylls shows an ideal society's inevitable dissolution; In Memoriam finds a framework for evolutionary time that can accommodate faith
  • Sound as meaning — Tennyson is the most musical poet in English after Milton; his metres, vowel sounds, and consonant patterns enact what the poem is saying; this is not ornament, it is argument
Faith and doubt — the Victorian crisis
The Victorian crisis of faith — produced by geological science (Lyell's Principles of Geology, 1830–33, which showed the earth was vastly older than the Bible implied), evolutionary theory (Darwin's Origin of Species, 1859), and higher biblical criticism — is the defining intellectual context for Tennyson's major work. In Memoriam was published in 1850, nine years before Darwin, but its "nature red in tooth and claw" passage already articulates the Darwinian problem: if nature operates by extinction, by the sacrifice of individuals for the preservation of species, what becomes of divine providence? What becomes of individual immortality? Tennyson's answer — "faintly trust the larger hope" — is explicitly not a logical demonstration; it is a willed trust in the face of unknowing.
Tennyson's sound — the technical secret
T.S. Eliot identified Tennyson's "impeccable ear" as his defining quality — and his thinking as secondary. This is too simple, but the technical observation is right. Tennyson's most famous effects are sonic: the slow vowels of "The moan of doves in immemorial elms, / And murmuring of innumerable bees" (The Princess); the relentless dactylic drive of "Half a league, half a league, half a league onward"; the enclosed sorrow of the In Memoriam stanza's ABBA rhyme. He spent enormous care on sound: he would find the right vowel sequence before the right word. This is not Romantic spontaneous overflow — it is craft of the highest order.
The pathetic fallacy — Victorian nature
Ruskin coined the term "pathetic fallacy" in Modern Painters (1856) to describe the practice of attributing human emotions to natural objects — "the cruel, crawling foam" — and named Tennyson as its exemplary practitioner. Tennyson's landscape is always psychological: the "blank day" breaking on the "bald street" in In Memoriam VII is grief made visible. The moated grange's decay in "Mariana" externalises her abandonment. The technique is both Tennyson's greatest strength (emotional immediacy) and his greatest critical vulnerability (accused of sentimentality, of projecting rather than perceiving).
Arthurian revival — why Tennyson chose Arthur
The Arthurian legend gave Tennyson an ideal framework: a society founded on high principles, in which the principles were betrayed from within. This is precisely the Victorian anxiety about progress — not external barbarism but internal moral failure. Arthur's knights do not fall to invasion but to adultery, greed, spiritual overreach (the Grail quest), and the impossibility of sustaining an ideal in fallen human nature. The choice of medieval setting gave Tennyson's critique of Victorian values a distance that made it publishable — and a romance that made it readable.
VIII

Critical Approaches

The critical pendulum — know these five positions
  • Victorian consensus: national poet — Tennyson was treated as Shakespeare's successor; his poems were memorised in schools; his moral authority was taken seriously
  • Modernist demolition: Eliot and Leavis — Eliot: perfect technique, thin thought; Leavis: Tennyson as the type of the poet who "didn't think"; this reduced Tennyson's academic standing catastrophically from 1920–1960
  • Mid-century rehabilitation: Ricks — Christopher Ricks's Tennyson (1972) demonstrated the complexity of the verbal texture; showed that the thinking was in the language, not separate from it
  • New Historicism: Tennyson as ideological — Alan Sinfield's Alfred Tennyson (1986); the poems as documents of Victorian ideology about class, gender, empire, and religion
  • Current: recovery and complexity — Tennyson is now read as more ambivalent, more ironic, and more self-aware than either his Victorian admirers or his Modernist detractors believed
T.S. Eliot (1936) "In Memoriam" essay: called it "the greatest poem of the 19th century" while simultaneously finding its thought thin — "the concentrated diary of a man confessing himself." Tennyson had "the finest ear of any English poet since Milton" — the praise and the implicit condescension in the same sentence.
F.R. Leavis Used Tennyson as the exemplary case of a poet who substituted sound for thought. Contrasted him unfavourably with Hopkins (also Victorian, but intellectually rigorous). This judgment, delivered with Leavis's authority, kept Tennyson out of serious academic study for decades.
Christopher Ricks (1972) Tennyson — the book that rehabilitated him. Ricks demonstrated through close reading that the thinking is embedded in the verbal texture — the syntax, the tense choices, the sound patterns. "Tennyson is a great poet, not in spite of his technique but because of it."
Alan Sinfield (1986) Alfred Tennyson — New Historicist reading. The poems as sites of Victorian ideological construction and contradiction. In Memoriam as a document of Victorian masculine friendship that could not name what it was; Idylls as Victorian imperialism dressed in medieval costume.
A.C. Bradley The Reaction Against Tennyson (1917) — written during the Modernist attack — defended Tennyson's philosophical seriousness and argued that the attacks confused subject matter with achievement.
John D. Rosenberg The Fall of Camelot (1973) — the essential modern study of Idylls of the King. Read the poem as an organically structured tragic epic rather than a loose allegorical collection.
Feminist criticism Mariana, Lady of Shalott, Elaine, Guinevere — all women defined by enclosure, waiting, or punishment for sexual transgression. Tennyson constructed a Victorian feminine ideal that simultaneously imprisoned and aestheticised women. Carol Christ, Gerhard Joseph.
Postcolonial "The Charge of the Light Brigade," Idylls's imperial allegory, "Locksley Hall"'s vision of "the Federation of the world" — all read through the lens of British imperial ideology. Matthew Rowlinson, Herbert Tucker.
IX

Literary & Historical Context

Tennyson's historical moment — the Victorian crises
  • The geological crisis (1830s) — Lyell's Principles of Geology showed the earth was millions of years old; Genesis was clearly not literal; In Memoriam's "nature red in tooth and claw" is the poetic response
  • The evolutionary crisis (1859) — Darwin's Origin of Species appeared the same year as Idylls' first instalment; Tennyson had anticipated it; he was the Victorian poet best equipped to respond because he had been thinking about it for 26 years
  • The Crimean War (1853–56) — produced "The Charge of the Light Brigade"; raised the question of what patriotic poetry could honestly say; Tennyson navigated it by praising the soldiers' courage without justifying the commanders' incompetence
  • The Great Exhibition (1851) — the same year as Tennyson's laureate appointment; the confidence of Victorian industrialism at its peak; the Idylls would spend 30 years questioning whether civilisation's material progress entailed moral progress
  • The Reform Acts (1832, 1867, 1884) — expanding the electorate; Tennyson worried about democracy and mob rule; "Locksley Hall Sixty Years After" is explicitly anxious about the direction of democratic politics
Tennyson and the Pre-Raphaelites
The Pre-Raphaelite Brotherhood — Rossetti, Holman Hunt, Millais, Burne-Jones — took Tennyson as their primary poetic source. "The Lady of Shalott," "Mariana," "The Palace of Art," and the Arthurian legends provided their subjects. Tennyson's medievalism, his sensuous descriptiveness, and his women defined by enclosure and beauty matched Pre-Raphaelite aesthetic priorities exactly. The relationship was reciprocal: the illustrations of Tennyson's Poems (1857) by Rossetti, Holman Hunt, and Millais are among the finest book illustrations of the century and in turn shaped how the poems were subsequently read and imagined.
Tennyson and Hopkins
Gerard Manley Hopkins — a Victorian contemporary largely unknown until 1918 — was in some respects Tennyson's opposite: where Tennyson's sound is smooth, mellifluous, and formally regular, Hopkins's is fractured, stressed, and experimentally violent. Yet both were obsessively technical; both believed sound was the primary carrier of poetic meaning; both were deeply preoccupied with faith. The contrast between them defines the range of Victorian poetic response to the same crises: Tennyson's provisional faith through beauty; Hopkins's agonised faith through sprung rhythm.
Influences on Tennyson
John Keats: The primary lyric influence — Tennyson said "Keats, with the exception of Shakespeare, is the greatest poet of the century." The sensuousness, the musical vowel-patterns, and the preoccupation with beauty-and-mortality are all Keatsian inheritances.
Virgil: The classical model for elevated epic — Tennyson translated portions of Virgil and the Virgilian pastoral informs the meditative mode of In Memoriam.
Milton: Blank verse mastery; the epic tradition; the Fall as structural model for Idylls.
Dante: The Commedia's structural journey through grief to provisional resolution is the model for In Memoriam's arc.
Byron: Early influence — the romantic hero, the narrative poem; Tennyson grew away from it but the early influence is visible in "Locksley Hall."
X

Key Critical Terms

In Memoriam stanza
Four lines of iambic tetrameter rhyming ABBA — the enclosed rhyme scheme enacted by Tennyson in In Memoriam A.H.H. The name is Tennyson's bequest to literary terminology. Not invented by him (appears in Sidney and Jonson) but made definitively his by 131 sustained sections of its use.
Dramatic monologue
A poem spoken by a single character whose speech reveals personality, psychology, and situation — often to an implied listener. Tennyson ("Ulysses," "Tithonus," "St. Simeon Stylites") and Browning ("My Last Duchess," "Fra Lippo Lippi") developed the form simultaneously in the 1830s–40s. The reader must judge the speaker's reliability.
Pathetic fallacy
Ruskin's term (coined in Modern Painters, 1856) for the attribution of human emotions to non-human things — particularly in natural description. "The cruel, crawling foam." Ruskin named Tennyson as its primary practitioner. Tennyson's external landscapes consistently mirror his speakers' internal states.
Idyll
From the Greek eidyllion — a short descriptive poem, typically pastoral or narrative. Tennyson's use in Idylls of the King is deliberately small-scale — each idyll is a narrative unit, not a full epic book. The title's modesty ("idylls," not "epic") is part of Tennyson's Victorian diffidence about claiming the grandest classical forms.
Monodrama
Tennyson's own term for Maud — a single speaker's psychological narrative told through a sequence of lyrics in different metres. Anticipates the dramatic lyric sequence of Browning and the stream-of-consciousness of Modernist fiction.
"Nature red in tooth and claw"
From In Memoriam, Section LVI — Tennyson's phrase for the amoral violence of natural selection as described by evolutionary science. Pre-dates Darwin's Origin of Species by nine years. The most quoted phrase from In Memoriam and one of the most quoted in Victorian intellectual history.
Elegy
A poem of mourning for the dead. The pastoral elegy convention (as in Milton's Lycidas) involves mourning, the questioning of divine justice, and consolation. In Memoriam is the greatest Victorian elegy and one of the greatest in English — unusual in its length, its sustained philosophical wrestling, and its refusal of easy consolation.
Laureate poetry
Poetry written in the official public role of Poet Laureate — for state occasions, national events, royal deaths and births. Tennyson's laureate poems: "Ode on the Death of the Duke of Wellington" (1852), "The Charge of the Light Brigade" (1854), "Ode on the Opening of the International Exhibition" (1862). The tension between the poet's private voice and the public role is a constant pressure in his career.
Blank verse
Unrhymed iambic pentameter — the metre of Paradise Lost, The Prelude, and Tennyson's "Ulysses," "Tithonus," "Morte d'Arthur," and the Idylls. Tennyson's blank verse is characterised by its vowel management — the care with which open and closed vowel sounds are distributed across the line to control pace and feeling.
Dactylic metre
A metrical foot of one stressed + two unstressed syllables (DUM-da-da). Used by Tennyson in "The Charge of the Light Brigade" to mimic the hoofbeat rhythm of the cavalry charge — "HALF a LEAGUE, HALF a LEAGUE, HALF a LEAGUE ON-ward." One of the most successful uses of mimetic metre in English poetry.

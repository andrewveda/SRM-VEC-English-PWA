I.

A Life in Epigrams

Renowned for his biting wit, flamboyant dress, and glittering conversational skill, Oscar Wilde became one of London's most popular playwrights in the early 1890s before his tragic downfall. AESTHETICISM
1854
Birth in Dublin
Born to a prominent eye-surgeon and a successful poet (known as 'Speranza'), Wilde was exposed to brilliance and eccentricity from a young age.
1874
Oxford Years
Attended Magdalen College, Oxford, where he excelled in Classics and became deeply involved in the rising philosophy of aestheticism (led by Walter Pater and John Ruskin).
1890
The Picture of Dorian Gray
Published his only novel, which was heavily criticized for its decadent themes but ultimately became a timeless literary classic.
1895
The Height & The Fall
The Importance of Being Earnest opened to massive success. However, the same year, his feud with the Marquess of Queensberry led to his infamous trials and subsequent imprisonment for "gross indecency."
1900
Exile & Death
Died destitute in Paris at the age of 46, reportedly saying of the terrible wallpaper in his room: "One of us will have to go."

II.

Theatrical Masterpieces

Comedy
The Importance of Being Earnest
1895 • Trivial Comedy for Serious People
▼
Overview
A farcical comedy in which the protagonists maintain fictitious personae to escape burdensome social obligations. It is Wilde's most enduringly popular play.
Tragedy
Salome
1891 • One-Act Tragedy
▼
Overview
Originally written in French, the play tells the biblical story of Salome, stepdaughter of the tetrarch Herod Antipas, who requests the head of Jokanaan (John the Baptist) on a silver platter.
Comedy
An Ideal Husband
1895 • Political Comedy
▼
Overview
A comedic stage play that revolves around blackmail and political corruption, touching on themes of public and private honor.
Comedy
Lady Windermere's Fan
1892 • Comedy of Manners
▼
Overview
A critique of the moral rigidities of Victorian society, detailing the story of Lady Windermere, who suspects her husband of having an affair with another woman.
III.

Aestheticism & Philosophy

"To reveal art and conceal the artist is art's aim... There is no such thing as a moral or an immoral book. Books are well written, or badly written. That is all."

— Preface to The Picture of Dorian Gray
Art for Art's SakeThe core tenet of the aesthetic movement.
Decadent MovementEmbracing artificiality over nature.
New HellenismRevival of ancient Greek ideals of beauty.
Key Vocabulary
Epigram
A pithy saying or remark expressing an idea in a clever and amusing way. Wilde was a master of turning Victorian platitudes upside down.
Dandy
A man unduly devoted to style, neatness, and fashion in dress and appearance. Wilde popularized this figure as a subversive social critic.
Farce
A comic dramatic work using buffoonery and horseplay and typically including crude characterization and ludicrously improbable situations.

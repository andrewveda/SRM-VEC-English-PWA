Percy Bysshe Shelley

1792 — 1822

"If Winter comes, can Spring be far behind?"

I

Life & Biography

Origins — the baronet's heir
Born 4 August 1792 at Field Place, Warnham, Sussex. The eldest son of Timothy Shelley, a Whig baronet and MP, and Elizabeth Pilfold. Shelley stood in line to inherit the baronetcy and an estate of £6,000 a year — a fortune that made his radical politics and voluntary poverty all the more striking. He was educated at Syon House Academy, then Eton College (1804–1810), where he was bullied for his refusal to participate in the fagging system, earned the nickname "Mad Shelley," and began his lifelong hatred of tyranny in all forms — beginning with institutional authority.
Oxford & expulsion
Entered University College, Oxford, in October 1810. Within months he had written and privately distributed a pamphlet: The Necessity of Atheism (1811) — the first openly atheist publication in English history. He refused to deny authorship before the college authorities and was expelled in March 1811, along with his friend Thomas Jefferson Hogg who had co-written the pamphlet. His father gave him an ultimatum to recant; Shelley refused. The expulsion, the estrangement from his father, and the loss of his inheritance prospect defined the next decade of his life financially.
First marriage — Harriet Westbrook
In August 1811, aged 18, Shelley eloped to Edinburgh with Harriet Westbrook, a sixteen-year-old schoolfellow of his sister's. He believed he was rescuing her from a tyrannical father. The marriage was genuinely affectionate initially; they had two children: Ianthe (1813) and Charles (1814). But by 1814 Shelley had fallen in love with Mary Godwin, daughter of the radical philosopher William Godwin and feminist Mary Wollstonecraft. He left Harriet. In December 1816, Harriet drowned herself in the Serpentine, London, heavily pregnant by another man. Shelley was devastated but also blamed. A Chancery court subsequently denied him custody of Ianthe and Charles — a judgment that haunted him.
Mary Godwin & the Godwin circle
Mary Godwin (1797–1851) was the daughter of William Godwin (Political Justice) and Mary Wollstonecraft (A Vindication of the Rights of Woman) — the most radical parentage imaginable. Shelley had been a devoted disciple of Godwin's philosophy for years before meeting his daughter. They eloped to Europe in July 1814, taking Mary's stepsister Claire Clairmont with them. The Godwin circle — Godwin himself (who disapproved of the elopement while borrowing money from Shelley), Hunt, Peacock, Hazlitt — was central to Shelley's intellectual and social world throughout the London years.
The Geneva summer (1816) — Villa Diodati
The summer of 1816 — the "Year Without a Summer" (caused by the eruption of Mount Tambora) — brought the Shelleys and Claire Clairmont to Geneva, where they stayed near Lord Byron at the Villa Diodati. The famous ghost-story competition on a stormy night produced Mary Shelley's Frankenstein and John Polidori's The Vampyre. Byron and Shelley sailed together on Lake Geneva; the conversations of that summer shaped Hymn to Intellectual Beauty and Mont Blanc. The relationship with Byron — admiring, competitive, and sometimes strained — continued for the rest of Shelley's life.
Italy (1818–1822) — the great years
The Shelleys left England permanently in March 1818 — Shelley never returned. They lived in Venice, Este, Rome, Livorno (Leghorn), Florence, Pisa, and finally Lerici. The Italian years produced almost all of Shelley's greatest work: Prometheus Unbound (1818–19), The Cenci (1819), Ode to the West Wind (1819), To a Skylark (1820), Adonais (1821), Epipsychidion (1821), Hellas (1822), The Triumph of Life (1822, unfinished). They were also years of tragedy: their infant daughter Clara died in 1818; their son William died in Rome in 1819. Only their son Percy Florence (born 1819) survived. The deaths of his children, financial anxiety, and Shelley's increasingly visionary isolation mark the Italian period.
Death — Lerici, 1822
On 1 July 1822, Shelley sailed from Livorno to Lerici with his friend Edward Williams in a small boat, the Don Juan. A sudden violent storm struck; the boat sank; both drowned. Shelley was 29. His body washed ashore ten days later. Byron, Leigh Hunt, and Edward John Trelawny cremated the body on the beach at Viareggio in a ceremony Trelawny later described as Shelley's heart refusing to burn — which he snatched from the flames. (The "heart" was probably the liver, calcified by disease, but the legend stuck.) The ashes were buried in the Protestant Cemetery in Rome, beside Keats's grave. His tombstone inscription: "Cor Cordium" (Heart of Hearts) and Ariel's song from The Tempest: "Nothing of him that doth fade, / But doth suffer a sea-change / Into something rich and strange."

II

Political & Philosophical Beliefs

Atheism & anti-religion
Shelley was the first major English poet to be openly and consistently atheist. The Necessity of Atheism (1811) argued that there is no rational evidence for God's existence. His atheism was not merely intellectual — it was political: he saw organised religion as a primary instrument of social oppression, supporting monarchy, aristocracy, and the subjugation of women and the poor. The notes to Queen Mab are extended attacks on Christianity. His later poems — especially Prometheus Unbound — present Jupiter (God-figure) as a tyrant to be overthrown. Even in poems of visionary beauty (Hymn to Intellectual Beauty), the "Spirit of Beauty" is explicitly not a Christian god.
Political radicalism — republicanism, anarchism, pacifism
Shelley's politics were to the left of all his contemporaries. Influenced by William Godwin's Political Justice (1793) — which argued that government, property, and marriage were all forms of tyranny — Shelley believed in the perfectibility of human nature once oppressive institutions were removed. He was a republican (opposed to monarchy), advocated Irish independence (visiting Dublin in 1812 to distribute pamphlets), opposed capital punishment, and was a vegetarian and animal rights supporter. After Peterloo (1819), he wrote his most explicitly political poems: The Mask of Anarchy (urging passive resistance) and Sonnet: England in 1819 (attacking George III, the Prince Regent, and the entire political establishment).
Platonism & idealism
Shelley read Plato in the original Greek and translated several dialogues, including the Symposium. Platonic idealism — the idea that the material world is a pale reflection of eternal, perfect Forms — pervades his poetry. Beauty is not in the world but glimpsed through the world; love reaches toward an absolute that no particular beloved can embody. This is the source of his recurrent imagery of veils, reflections, mirrors, and shadows: "Life, like a dome of many-coloured glass, / Stains the white radiance of Eternity" (Adonais). The world is a veil over the absolute; the poet tears it aside.
Free love & the critique of marriage
Following Godwin's Political Justice, Shelley believed that legal marriage was a form of property right over women — incompatible with genuine love, which must be free. His own life enacted this belief: he did not consider himself unfaithful to Harriet in loving Mary; he regarded Claire Clairmont's (and later Teresa Viviani's and Jane Williams's) emotional claims as legitimate. Epipsychidion is the most extreme poetic statement of this position: the beloved is a spiritual ideal that cannot be possessed by any single relationship. Victorian critics (Matthew Arnold) found this personally scandalous and used it to limit his canonical status.
The role of the poet — "unacknowledged legislators"
The most famous claim in A Defence of Poetry: "Poets are the unacknowledged legislators of the World." This is not modesty — Shelley argues that poets shape the fundamental imaginative categories through which humanity understands itself. Before law, before philosophy, before religion, there is the poem — which creates the sympathetic imagination that makes justice and community possible. The political and aesthetic are not separate for Shelley: to expand the imagination is to advance human liberty. This is the ultimate Romantic claim for poetry's social function.
III

Major Lyric Poems

Ode to the West Wind NET HIGH
October 1819 · Florence · 5 cantos, terza rima · Published 1820
▼
Opening canto
O wild West Wind, thou breath of Autumn's being, Thou, from whose unseen presence the leaves dead Are driven, like ghosts from an enchanter fleeing, Yellow, and black, and pale, and hectic red, Pestilence-stricken multitudes: O thou, Who chariotest to their dark wintry bed The wingèd seeds, where they lie cold and low, Each like a corpse within its grave, until Thine azure sister of the Spring shall blow Her clarion o'er the dreaming earth, and fill (Driving sweet buds like flocks to feed in air) With living hues and odours plain and hill: Wild Spirit, which art moving everywhere; Destroyer and Preserver; hear, oh hear!
Structure
Five fourteen-line cantos, each in terza rima (ABA BCB CDC DED EE) — Dante's interlocking rhyme scheme, appropriate for a poem about the interconnectedness of all things. Each canto ends with a couplet. The first three cantos address the West Wind's power over leaves (earth), clouds (sky), and sea respectively. The fourth canto is the speaker's self-identification with the Wind's power in his youth; the fifth is the climactic prayer: make me your lyre.
The pivotal canto IV — the humiliation of the self
If I were a dead leaf thou mightest bear; If I were a swift cloud to fly with thee; A wave to pant beneath thy power, and share The impulse of thy strength, only less free Than thou, O uncontrollable! Even I were as in my boyhood, and could be The comrade of thy wanderings over Heaven, As then, when to outstrip thy skiey speed Scarce seemed a vision; I have never given As thus with thee in prayer in my sore need. Oh! lift me as a wave, a leaf, a cloud! I fall upon the thorns of life! I bleed!
Critical themes
• Destroyer and Preserver: The wind is simultaneously destructive (stripping leaves, driving storms) and creative (carrying seeds for spring, inspiring poetry). Shelley refuses to separate creation from destruction — the fallen leaves are necessary for the new growth.
• The poet as Aeolian lyre: The final canto's prayer — "Make me thy lyre" — invokes the Aeolian harp, an instrument that makes music when wind passes through it. The poet does not control inspiration; he is the instrument through which the Wind (nature, the Absolute, the revolutionary spirit) speaks.
• Political allegory: Written in October 1819, immediately after Peterloo (August 1819). The "unextinguished hearth" and "ashes and sparks" of the final lines are explicitly political — "Scatter, as from an unextinguished hearth / Ashes and sparks, my words among mankind!" The poem is a prayer for revolutionary prophecy.
• "If Winter comes, can Spring be far behind?": The most famous last line in Shelley — the cyclical structure of the ode (autumn leads to spring) embeds a political optimism: the current winter of oppression will give way to a revolutionary spring. This is not naive — Shelley knows the winter is real and painful.
Form significance
The choice of terza rima — Dante's interlocking form — is deliberate. Each rhyme depends on what came before and leads to what follows; no stanza is self-contained. The form enacts the poem's argument: all things are connected; the Wind that strips the leaves also carries the seeds; destruction and creation are one. Shelley was the first English poet to use terza rima for a major poem.
Ozymandias NET HIGH
1817 · Published 1818 in The Examiner · Sonnet
▼
I met a traveller from an antique land, Who said — "Two vast and trunkless legs of stone Stand in the desert... Near them, on the sand, Half sunk a shattered visage lies, whose frown, And wrinkled lip, and sneer of cold command, Tell that its sculptor well those passions read Which yet survive, stamped on these lifeless things, The hand that mocked them, and the heart that fed; And on the pedestal, these words appear: My name is Ozymandias, King of Kings; Look on my Works, ye Mighty, and despair! Nothing beside remains. Round the decay Of that colossal Wreck, boundless and bare The lone and level sands stretch far away."
Critical analysis
• Written as a competition: Composed in friendly competition with Horace Smith, who wrote a poem on the same subject simultaneously. Shelley's was published in The Examiner (January 1818).
• The historical figure: Ozymandias = Ramesses II (Ramesses the Great), Egyptian pharaoh. The British Museum was acquiring Egyptian antiquities in this period; the "Younger Memnon" bust of Ramesses arrived in 1821. Shelley was writing in a specific cultural moment of fascination with Egyptian power and its ruins.
• The irony: The inscription — "Look on my Works, ye Mighty, and despair!" — now commands the desert emptiness. "Nothing beside remains." The tyrant's boast survives; the works do not. The sculpture's survival is also ironic — the "sculptor well those passions read" — the artist outlasts the king.
• Anti-tyranny: The poem is a direct political statement: all political power is temporary. Written in the context of the post-Napoleonic Restoration — the reactionary monarchies reasserting themselves across Europe — the poem argues that their power, like Ozymandias's, will pass.
• Sonnet form: Unusual rhyme scheme: ABAB ACDC EDE FFE — not quite Petrarchan, not quite Shakespearean. The dissonances in the form mirror the fragmentation of the monument.
To a Skylark NET HIGH
June 1820 · Livorno · 21 stanzas · Published 1820
▼
Hail to thee, blithe Spirit! Bird thou never wert, That from Heaven, or near it, Pourest thy full heart In profuse strains of unpremeditated art.
Structure & argument
21 stanzas of four short lines plus one longer line — a rising, accelerating form that mimes the skylark's ascent. The skylark is addressed as a "blithe Spirit" — never a bird, always something else. The poem proceeds through a series of metaphors for what the skylark "is like": a poet hidden by light; a maiden in a tower; a glowworm in a dell; a rose embowered. None suffices — the skylark exceeds all comparison.

The central question: what is the source of the skylark's joy? The skylark does not know sorrow, satiety, or death. Human beings — who do know these things — can only approximate its song. The poem ends with a prayer: if I could learn a fraction of your gladness, "The world should listen then, as I am listening now."
Comparison with Keats's Nightingale
The comparison is irresistible and has been a staple of NET questions. Both poems address a bird that represents something beyond the human. But:
• Keats's nightingale sings in darkness, at night, associated with mortality and dissolution — "now more than ever seems it rich to die"
• Shelley's skylark sings in full daylight, at height, associated with pure unreflective joy — "blithe Spirit," unpremeditated
• Keats ends in uncertainty and return: "Do I wake or sleep?" The imagination cannot hold
• Shelley ends in aspiration: "Teach me half the gladness / That thy brain must know" — the poet remains below, reaching upward
• Keats's bird is mortal (it will be heard "in ancient days by emperor and clown"); Shelley's is "never wert" a bird — it transcends the material entirely
Hymn to Intellectual Beauty
Summer 1816 · Geneva · 7 stanzas
▼
Significance
Written in the Geneva summer alongside Mont Blanc — the two poems are companion pieces written after sailing with Byron on Lake Geneva. Where Mont Blanc addresses the sublime indifference of natural power, Hymn to Intellectual Beauty addresses the fleeting visitations of the "Spirit of Beauty" — a secular substitute for religious experience. "The name of God and ghosts and Heaven" remain "frail spells" — the Spirit of Beauty is the real source of meaning. Autobiographical: stanza 6 describes Shelley's adolescent vow to dedicate himself to the Spirit of Beauty.
Mont Blanc
Summer 1816 · Chamonix · Irregular blank verse · Published 1817
▼
Critical significance
Written in the presence of the mountain, Shelley's most philosophically demanding poem. It meditates on the relationship between the human mind and nature — specifically on whether "Power" (the glacial force that shapes and destroys) is mindful or mindless. The poem does not resolve the question: "And what were thou, and earth, and stars, and sea, / If to the human mind's imaginings / Silence and solitude were vacancy?" The mind may project meaning onto the mountain; or the mountain may possess independent Power. Shelley refuses to decide — an unusual moment of Negative Capability in a poet usually more assertive than Keats.
The Mask of Anarchy Political
September 1819 · Written after Peterloo · Not published until 1832
▼
Context & argument
Written in a white heat after hearing of the Peterloo Massacre (16 August 1819) — Shelley was in Italy and received the news from Leigh Hunt. The poem was sent immediately to Hunt for publication in The Examiner; Hunt delayed publication for twelve years, fearing prosecution. Not published until 1832 — after the Reform Act.

The poem is a political allegory in the ballad tradition: Murder (Castlereagh — Foreign Secretary), Fraud (Eldon — Lord Chancellor), Hypocrisy (Sidmouth — Home Secretary), and Anarchy (representing tyrannical government itself) ride triumphant through England. Then: a female figure, Hope (England), is about to be crushed — but a "mantle" falls over her. A voice rises from the ground: "Rise like Lions after slumber / In unvanquishable number — / Shake your chains to earth like dew / Which in sleep had fallen on you — / Ye are many — they are few."
The paradox — passive resistance
Despite its fierce tone, the poem advocates non-violent passive resistance — not armed revolution. The crowd should stand still and let the troops ride them down, so that the world witnesses tyranny in action. This proto-Gandhian argument makes The Mask of Anarchy remarkable among political poems. Gandhi quoted it; it influenced the civil rights movement.
Key stanza
Rise like Lions after slumber In unvanquishable number— Shake your chains to earth like dew Which in sleep had fallen on you— Ye are many — they are few.
England in 1819 NET Trap
1819 · Unpublished in Shelley's lifetime · Petrarchan sonnet
▼
An old, mad, blind, despised, and dying King — Princes, the dregs of their dull race, who flow Through public scorn — mud from a muddy spring — Rulers who neither see, nor feel, nor know, But leech-like to their fainting country cling Till they drop, blind in blood, without a blow — A people starved and stabbed in th' untilled field — An army, which liberticide and prey Makes as a two-edged sword to all who wield — Golden and sanguine laws which tempt and slay; Religion Christless, Godless — a book sealed; A Senate — Time's worst statute unrepealed — Are graves from which a glorious Phantom may Burst, to illumine our tempestuous day.
Analysis
Thirteen lines of denunciation, one line of hope. The "old, mad, blind" king is George III (mad by this date, dying in 1820). The "Princes" are the Prince Regent and his brothers. "A people starved and stabbed" — Peterloo. The poem withholds its turn (the Petrarchan volta) until the final couplet — the "glorious Phantom" of liberty that might yet rise from the graves of oppression. Not published in Shelley's lifetime — too dangerous. First published by Mary Shelley in Posthumous Poems (1824).
Adonais — Elegy for Keats NET HIGH
June 1821 · Pisa · 55 Spenserian stanzas · Published 1821
▼
Genre & model
A pastoral elegy in the classical tradition — modelled on Bion's Lament for Adonis and Moschus's Lament for Bion, and more immediately on Milton's Lycidas. The name "Adonais" combines Adonis (the beautiful youth killed in the myth) with the Hebrew name for God (Adonai). The deceased is Keats; the poem publicly blames the Quarterly Review and Blackwood's for killing him with hostile criticism.
The famous stanzas
He is made one with Nature: there is heard His voice in all her music, from the moan Of thunder, to the song of night's sweet bird; He is a portion of the loveliness Which once he made more lovely...
The One remains, the many change and pass; Heaven's light forever shines, Earth's shadows fly; Life, like a dome of many-coloured glass, Stains the white radiance of Eternity, Until Death tramples it to fragments.
The movement of the elegy
The poem moves through three phases typical of pastoral elegy:
Mourning (I–XVII): Urania (the muse of astronomy / heavenly poetry, here representing Venus-Urania, divine love) is called to mourn; the fellow poets weep; Spring returns but Adonais does not.
Consolation (XVIII–XXXVIII): The mourners arrive — Byron ("the Pilgrim of Eternity"), Thomas Moore ("sweetest lyrist"), and Shelley himself ("a frail Form, / A phantom among men; companionless").
Transcendence (XXXIX–LV): Adonais has not truly died — he has become part of the eternal One. The poem ends with Shelley's own soul being drawn toward the same dissolution: "I am borne darkly, fearfully, afar."
Controversy
Shelley blames the Quarterly Review for Keats's death — "Our Adonais has drunk poison; oh! / What deaf and viperous murderer could crown / Life's early cup with such a draught of woe?" Byron and Keats himself rejected this narrative. The myth "killed by a review" was created and sustained largely by Adonais. Byron satirised it in Don Juan: "Poor fellow! His was an untoward fate— / 'T is strange the mind, that very fiery particle, / Should let itself be snuffed out by an article." Shelley knew the myth was somewhat exaggerated but found it politically useful as an indictment of the Tory press.
The Cloud
1820 · Published 1820 · 6 stanzas
▼
Significance
One of Shelley's most playful and technically dazzling poems — spoken in the first person by the Cloud itself. The Cloud describes the water cycle in scientifically accurate terms (evaporation, condensation, lightning, rain) but in language of mythological extravagance. The final stanza's paradox: "I am the daughter of Earth and Water, / And the nursling of the Sky; / I pass through the pores of the ocean and shores; / I change, but I cannot die." Change without extinction — the cloud is immortal because it perpetually transforms. This is Shelley's counter-image to mortality: not permanence but endless metamorphosis.
Mutability
1814 · Published 1816 · Sonnet
▼
We are as clouds that veil the midnight moon; How restlessly they speed, and gleam, and quiver, Streaking the darkness radiantly! — yet soon Night closes round, and they are lost forever: Or like forgotten lyres, whose dissonant strings Give various response to each varying blast, To whose frail frame no second motion brings One mood or modulation like the last. We rest. — A dream has power to poison sleep; We rise. — One wandering thought pollutes the day; We feel, conceive or reason, laugh or weep; Embrace fond woe, or cast our cares away: It is the same! — For, be it joy or sorrow, The path of its departure still is free; Man's yesterday may ne'er be like his morrow; Nought may endure but Mutability.
Significance
An early but perfect expression of Shelley's central philosophical preoccupation: everything changes, nothing is stable, the self is discontinuous — and the only permanent principle is change itself. The Aeolian lyre image recurs: we are "forgotten lyres" whose response varies with every passing wind. Not a lament but a statement of metaphysical fact — the tone is strangely equable.
IV

Dramatic Works

Prometheus Unbound NET HIGH
1818–19 · Published 1820 · Lyrical drama in 4 acts
▼
The myth — and Shelley's radical revision
In Aeschylus's lost Prometheus Unbound, the Titan Prometheus (who gave fire to humanity and was chained to a rock by Jupiter) was eventually reconciled with Jupiter — released in exchange for revealing a prophecy. Shelley explicitly rejects this ending: Prometheus should not submit. His Prometheus refuses to revoke his curse on Jupiter even after millennia of torture. Jupiter falls through his own internal contradictions; Prometheus is freed not by compromising with power but by the power being destroyed from within.
Act-by-act summary
Act I: Prometheus, chained on the Caucasian mountains, recalls the curse he pronounced on Jupiter. He asks to hear it repeated — but when he does, he no longer endorses it: "I wish no living thing to suffer pain." This withdrawal of hatred is the pivot of the drama. Phantasms, Furies, and voices of hope visit him. Asia and Panthea (his companions) are absent — driven away by Jupiter's tyranny.
Act II: Asia and Panthea journey to Demogorgon's cave. Demogorgon (a name for the "primal Power" or the Absolute) tells Asia that Jupiter's reign will end when "the hour is come" — the hour of human will finally asserting itself. Asia undergoes a transformation; she becomes "A Shape of light" — love itself becoming active.
Act III: Jupiter falls — Demogorgon rises from the abyss and hurls Jupiter into the darkness. Hercules unbinds Prometheus. Earth rejoices; the Spirit of the Hour is sent to proclaim the transformation to humanity.
Act IV: A choral celebration of the liberated universe — Earth, Moon, spirits, hours. The most lyrical and difficult act. Demogorgon delivers the closing invocation: "To suffer woes which Hope thinks infinite; / To forgive wrongs darker than death or night; / To defy Power, which seems omnipotent; / To love, and bear; to hope till Hope creates / From its own wreck the thing it contemplates."
Critical themes
• Political allegory: Jupiter = tyranny (the combined power of monarchy, aristocracy, and religion). Prometheus = liberated humanity. The poem is a revolutionary vision: not armed revolution but an internal transformation of consciousness that makes tyranny impossible.
• Love as the revolutionary force: Asia represents love — the active principle that can transform the world. This is Shelley's Platonic inheritance: Eros as the force that drives toward the Good.
• The problem of evil: Jupiter does not fall through external force but through the withdrawal of Prometheus's submission. Once humanity refuses to participate in its own oppression, tyranny collapses. This is the poem's most radical — and most idealistic — claim.
• "Lyrical drama": Not intended for performance — Shelley calls it a "lyrical drama" to distinguish it from stage drama. It is a drama of ideas, written in extraordinary lyric verse. The genre is closer to Milton's Samson Agonistes or Goethe's Faust than to stage tragedy.
Key quotations

"I gave all / He has; and in return he chains me here / Years, ages, night and day... / ...I would fain / Be what it is my destiny to be, / The saviour and the strength of suffering man."

— Prometheus, Act I

"The loathsome mask has fallen, the man remains / Sceptreless, free, uncircumscribed, but man / Equal, unclassed, tribeless, and nationless."

— Spirit of the Hour, Act III
The Cenci Stage Tragedy
1819 · Published 1819 · Five-act tragedy in blank verse
▼
Historical basis
Based on a true story from Renaissance Rome: Francesco Cenci, a vicious and powerful nobleman, rapes his daughter Beatrice. She organises his murder. She and other family members are arrested, tortured, and executed — despite widespread sympathy for their situation. Shelley read the account in an Italian manuscript and saw a portrait of Beatrice Cenci (attributed to Guido Reni) in Rome. He wanted to write a play that could actually be staged — unlike Prometheus Unbound.
Significance
The Cenci is Shelley's most successful attempt at conventional dramatic form. It is widely considered the best closet drama of the Romantic period (and arguably the last major English tragedy in blank verse before the Victorian era). Its theme — the corruption of institutional authority (the Church, the law, the family patriarch) and its destruction of innocence — is the same as Prometheus Unbound, but in psychological rather than mythological terms. The play was not staged in England until 1922 (due to the incest theme); it was performed by the Shelley Society in 1886 in a private performance.
Hellas
1821–22 · Published 1822 · Lyrical drama
▼
Context & significance
Written in support of the Greek War of Independence (1821) against Ottoman rule. Modelled on Aeschylus's Persians. Mahmud, the Ottoman Sultan, receives news of the Greek revolt through visions and choruses. Shelley was fervently philhellenic — Greece represented the origin of democratic and artistic civilisation, now fighting for its freedom. The famous final chorus: "The world's great age begins anew, / The golden years return." Contains the oft-quoted couplet: "The world is weary of the past, / Oh, might it die or rest at last!"
V

Narrative Poems

Alastor, or the Spirit of Solitude
1815 · Published 1816 · Blank verse narrative
▼
Argument & significance
A young poet (unnamed, the "Alastor" figure) travels the world in search of ideal beauty — seeking a vision of the perfect beloved seen in a dream. Unable to find her in the real world, he withdraws from human community and dies in solitude. The title "Alastor" means an avenging spirit in Greek — but Shelley's preface complicates the moral: the poet is both condemned (for narcissistic withdrawal from human sympathy) and celebrated (for his uncompromising idealism). The poem is a self-critical work: Shelley diagnoses a danger within his own temperament.
Queen Mab
1813 · Printed privately · 9 cantos with prose notes
▼
Significance
Shelley's first major long poem — a visionary philosophical poem in which the fairy Queen Mab shows the sleeping soul of Ianthe the past, present, and future of humanity. The prose notes are as important as the poem — they contain Shelley's most direct statements of atheism, vegetarianism, free love, and political radicalism. Shelley tried to suppress it later; it circulated in pirate editions and was enormously popular with working-class radical movements throughout the 19th century. The Chartists loved it. Richard Carlile republished it as a radical tract. It is the only Shelley text that was genuinely read by the working class in his lifetime.
Epipsychidion
1821 · Published 1821 · 604 lines
▼
Context & argument
Written in a state of visionary excitement about Teresa Viviani ("Emily"), a young Italian woman imprisoned in a convent by her father while awaiting marriage. Shelley identified her with the ideal of beauty he had always sought. The poem is an extended meditation on love as cosmic force — going far beyond the personal. Mary Shelley (a real person) appears as the "cold chaste Moon"; Emily/Teresa is the Sun (the ideal); other women in his life are stars. The poem argues for love's multiplicity: "True Love in this differs from gold and clay, / That to divide is not to take away." Shelley later disowned it: "It is an idealised history of my life and feelings. I think one is always in love with something or other; the error — and I confess it is not easy for spirits cased in flesh and blood to avoid it — consists in seeking in a mortal image the likeness of what is perhaps eternal."
The Triumph of Life (unfinished)
1822 · Incomplete at his death · Terza rima
▼
The last poem
The poem Shelley was writing when he drowned — found in his pocket. A dark, Dantesque vision modelled on Petrarch's Triumphs. The narrator sees a great chariot of Life crushing all before it — even the great figures of human history (Napoleon, Voltaire) are dragged in its wake. Only those who refused to "mix" with life — figures of pure contemplation — are spared. The guide-figure is Rousseau, who describes how he was seduced by the "Shape all light" (ideal beauty) and then destroyed by "Life." The poem breaks off mid-sentence. It is Shelley's most pessimistic and most Keatsian work — the visionary optimism of Prometheus Unbound seems to have collapsed. Paul de Man's essay on The Triumph of Life is one of the most important deconstructive readings in Romantic criticism.
VI

Prose Works

A Defence of Poetry NET HIGH
Written 1821 · Published posthumously 1840
▼
Context — Peacock's challenge
Written as a response to Thomas Love Peacock's satirical essay The Four Ages of Poetry (1820), which argued that poetry was a relic of barbarous primitive culture — that the age of reason and science had superseded the need for poetry. Peacock argued that poets were "semi-barbarians in a civilised community" who cultivated feeling at the expense of reason. Shelley's Defence is a passionate counter-argument.
Key arguments
1. Poetry and the imagination: Poetry is the expression of the imagination — and the imagination is the faculty that produces the sympathetic identification with others that makes moral life possible. Poetry is therefore prior to ethics, law, and philosophy — it creates the conditions for all of them.

2. "Poets are the unacknowledged legislators of the World": The final sentence. Poets shape the imaginative categories through which humanity understands itself, love, justice, and freedom. They "legislate" not through explicit argument but through expanding the sympathetic imagination.

3. The "two kinds of mental action": Reason (analysis, which divides) and Imagination (synthesis, which connects). Poetry belongs to imagination; it is therefore the faculty of sympathy and unity.

4. "Poetry is a mirror which makes beautiful that which is distorted": Art does not merely reflect — it transforms, idealises, creates.

5. The poet as creator of the language of the future: "Poets are the hierophants of an unapprehended inspiration; the mirrors of the gigantic shadows which futurity casts upon the present."
Famous sentences

"Poetry is the record of the best and happiest moments of the happiest and best minds."

— A Defence of Poetry

"Poets are the unacknowledged legislators of the World."

— Final sentence of A Defence of Poetry

"A poem is the very image of life expressed in its eternal truth."

NET trap: A Defence of Poetry was written in 1821 but not published until 1840 — twenty years after Shelley's death. Peacock's The Four Ages of Poetry (1820) is the text it responds to. Students often confuse the date of writing with the date of publication.
The Necessity of Atheism (1811)
The pamphlet that got Shelley expelled from Oxford. A short, logical argument that the existence of God cannot be demonstrated by sense, reason, or testimony — therefore belief in God is unjustifiable. Written with Thomas Jefferson Hogg. Sent anonymously to Oxford heads of house and bishops. The college authorities called Shelley before them; he refused to deny authorship or recant; he was expelled. The pamphlet has a historically significant title — it is arguably the first openly atheist publication in the English language by a named (though pseudonymous) author.
An Address to the Irish People (1812)
Written and distributed in Dublin during Shelley's first political campaign — advocating for Catholic emancipation and the repeal of the Act of Union. Shelley stood on a balcony and threw copies to passers-by. The pamphlet is notable for its advocacy of peaceful, gradual reform rather than armed revolution — evidence that Shelley's political pacifism preceded The Mask of Anarchy by seven years.
VII

Major Themes

Liberty & tyranny
The central and most consistent theme across all of Shelley's work — from Queen Mab (1813) to The Triumph of Life (1822). Tyranny appears in all its forms: political (Jupiter, the English monarchy, the Ottoman Empire), religious (Christianity as an instrument of oppression), domestic (the patriarchal family in The Cenci), intellectual (the dogmas that prevent free thought). Liberty is not merely political but spiritual — the freedom of the imagination and the will. Prometheus refuses to submit; Beatrice Cenci resists her father; Hellas celebrates Greek independence. Shelley is the most consistently political of the major Romantic poets.
The ideal & the real — Platonism
The tension between the ideal (eternal, perfect, invisible) and the real (material, imperfect, visible) pervades Shelley's poetry. The "Spirit of Beauty" in Hymn to Intellectual Beauty visits and departs; the skylark transcends the material world entirely; Asia in Prometheus Unbound becomes pure light; Adonais dissolves into the "One." The persistent imagery of veils, shadows, reflections, and domes of glass articulates a Platonic metaphysics: the world is a veil over the real. The poet's job is to pierce it — or to help readers see through it.
Mutability & impermanence
The sonnet "Mutability" (1816) states the theme most baldly: "Nought may endure but Mutability." Unlike Keats, who responds to impermanence with sensuousness and elegy, Shelley tends to respond with assertion — the Wind destroys AND preserves; the Cloud changes but cannot die; Adonais has not died but become part of the eternal One. Change is both the condition of suffering and the condition of hope — the same force that topples tyrants also makes beauty transient.
Love as cosmic force
For Shelley, love is never merely personal — it is the cosmic principle that connects all things. Asia in Prometheus Unbound is love made active; Epipsychidion is an extended meditation on love's multiplicity. Love is the opposite of the self-enclosure of tyranny — it reaches outward, identifies with the other, dissolves individual boundaries. This Platonic-Eros is both the most ambitious and most problematic aspect of Shelley's thought: in practice, his relationships were often painful for the women involved (Harriet, Mary, Fanny Imlay).
The poet as prophet & outcast
Shelley's poet figures are simultaneously visionary (able to see what others cannot) and marginal (unacknowledged, exiled, misunderstood). The Alastor figure dies in solitude; Shelley self-portraits as a "frail Form" in Adonais; the "unacknowledged legislators" are unacknowledged. This combination — of prophetic confidence and social isolation — is the defining condition of Shelley's self-image as a poet. He genuinely believed his work would transform the world; he also genuinely lived as a social outcast, unread in his lifetime.
Nature — not Wordsworthian
Shelley's nature is not Wordsworth's "spots of time" — it does not speak directly to the human soul in a reciprocal exchange. Shelley's natural forces (the West Wind, Mont Blanc, the skylark) are sublime precisely in their indifference to human categories. The Wind does not care about Shelley's suffering — which is why the prayer "Make me thy lyre!" is so desperate. The question of whether nature has an inherent spirit (pantheism) or is simply Power (materialism) is one that Shelley explicitly refuses to resolve — especially in Mont Blanc.
VIII

Critical Approaches

Matthew Arnold (19th c.) Called Shelley "a beautiful and ineffectual angel, beating in the void his luminous wings in vain." Admired the lyric beauty but found the ideas impractical and the life morally objectionable. This reading dominated Victorian reception and limited Shelley's canonical standing.
F.R. Leavis (20th c.) Revaluation (1936): Severe attack on Shelley's intellectual vagueness and verbal inflation. "The Shelleyan imagery does not evoke the object — it dissolves it." Leavis preferred the concrete sensuousness of Keats and the metaphysical wit of Donne. This attack reduced Shelley's standing mid-century.
Earl Wasserman Shelley: A Critical Reading (1971): Rehabilitated Shelley as a serious philosophical poet. Read the major poems through Platonic and sceptical philosophy. The definitive scholarly defence against Leavis.
New Historicism (Marilyn Butler) Romantics, Rebels and Reactionaries (1981): Read Shelley's radicalism in its historical context — Peterloo, the Reform crisis, the post-Napoleonic reaction. Restored the political dimension that Arnold and Leavis had suppressed.
Paul de Man (Deconstruction) Essay on The Triumph of Life: Shelley's final poem undoes its own idealist claims — the poem "performs" the failure of the vision it describes. A deconstructive reading that uses Shelley to question Romantic ideology.
Feminist criticism Barbara Charlesworth Gelpi, Anne Mellor. Examined Shelley's relationships with women (Harriet, Mary, Claire) and the gender dynamics of his poetry. Shelley presented himself as a liberator of women; feminist critics ask whether his "free love" philosophy served women's interests or his own.
Postcolonial Shelley's philhellenism (Hellas) and Irish interventionism read as expressions of a particular European cultural imperialism — even as liberation movements. His relationship with "the Orient" (as backdrop in Alastor, The Revolt of Islam) examined through Said's Orientalism framework.
Ecocriticism Recent readings of Mont Blanc, Ode to the West Wind, and The Cloud as early meditations on the relationship between human culture and natural forces — relevant to climate discourse.
IX

Literary & Historical Context

The Second Generation Romantics
Shelley, Keats, and Byron — the "Second Generation" — came of age when the French Revolution had collapsed into Napoleonic empire and Tory restoration. Where Wordsworth and Coleridge retreated from political radicalism after the Revolution's failure, the Second Generation maintained or intensified it. Shelley is the most consistently radical of the three: Byron was a radical aristocrat who enjoyed the game; Keats was primarily aesthetic rather than political; Shelley subordinated everything — personal life, career, poetic style — to his political and philosophical convictions.
The Peterloo Massacre & political poetry
16 August 1819: Government cavalry charged a crowd of 60,000–80,000 gathered at St Peter's Field, Manchester, to demand parliamentary reform. 15 killed; 400–700 injured. The name "Peterloo" mocked the heroic Waterloo. Shelley wrote The Mask of Anarchy and Sonnet: England in 1819 in immediate response. The political radicalism of 1819 — the year of the great odes for Keats, the revolutionary lyrics for Shelley — makes this one of the most politically charged years in English literary history.
Influences on Shelley
William Godwin — Political Justice (1793): The central philosophical influence. Anarchist, rationalist, perfectibilist.
Mary Wollstonecraft — A Vindication of the Rights of Woman (1792): Feminist political philosophy; Shelley's father-in-law's first wife.
Plato — Symposium, Phaedrus, Ion: Shelley translated several dialogues. Platonic idealism is the metaphysical foundation.
Milton — Paradise Lost: Shelley claimed Satan was Milton's true hero — the rebel against divine tyranny. Prometheus is his own Satan.
Dante — Divina Commedia: Structural and metaphysical influence, especially on terza rima usage and the journey-vision form.
Aeschylus — Prometheus Bound: The direct model and foil for Prometheus Unbound.
Leigh Hunt — Editor, mentor, and political comrade. Published many of Shelley's poems in The Examiner.
X

Key Critical Terms

Terza rima
Interlocking three-line rhyme scheme: ABA BCB CDC DED… Used by Dante in the Commedia. Shelley uses it in Ode to the West Wind — the first major English poem in this form — and in The Triumph of Life. The interlocking structure enacts connection and inevitability.
Lyrical drama
Shelley's own genre designation for Prometheus Unbound — a dramatic form not intended for stage performance, in which lyric intensity takes precedence over theatrical action. Closer to Milton's Samson Agonistes or Goethe's Faust. Influenced by the Greek chorus tradition.
The Aeolian lyre
A stringed instrument placed in a window or outdoors to make music when wind passes through it. Used by Shelley (West Wind, Mutability) and Coleridge (The Eolian Harp) as a central metaphor for inspiration: the poet as passive instrument through which natural/divine force speaks. Shelley uses it more ambivalently than Coleridge — "Make me thy lyre" is a desperate prayer, not a comfortable description.
Egotistical sublime
Keats's term (used of Wordsworth) for the poetic mode in which the poet's self dominates — all experience filtered through the ego. Relevant to Shelley because he occupies an uncomfortable position: he advocates Keatsian self-dissolution in theory but his poetry is frequently more assertive and self-centred than Keats's.
Demogorgon
The enigmatic figure in Prometheus Unbound who overthrows Jupiter. His name combines "Demos" (the people) and "Gorgon" (a terrifying face). He represents the absolute, unconscious power of necessity — neither good nor evil, simply the force that makes change possible when the time comes. Shelley deliberately makes him incomprehensible to resist any fixed interpretation.
Philhellenism
Love of ancient Greek culture and support for modern Greek independence. Shelley (Hellas), Byron (who died fighting for Greek independence), and Keats (the Elgin Marbles sonnets) all expressed philhellenic sentiment. It was the culturally dominant political cause of the 1820s for liberal Romantics.
Unacknowledged legislators
The famous final phrase of A Defence of Poetry: "Poets are the unacknowledged legislators of the World." Shelley's argument: poets shape the imaginative categories that make law, justice, and community possible. The "unacknowledged" is crucial — poets exercise enormous cultural power without being recognised as doing so.
Closet drama
Drama written to be read rather than staged. The Cenci is often cited as the finest closet drama of the Romantic period. Most Romantic drama (Byron's Manfred, Shelley's Prometheus Unbound) falls into this category — the theatre of the period could not accommodate such works, and the authors often did not intend them for performance.
Pastoral elegy
A classical genre: an elegy for the death of a fellow poet or person of talent, cast in pastoral (countryside/shepherd) terms. The convention involves mourning, blaming, and consolation through nature's renewal or transcendence. Shelley's Adonais follows Milton's Lycidas (which follows Bion and Moschus) in this tradition.
Perfectibilism
The belief (derived from Godwin's Political Justice) that human nature is capable of indefinite improvement — that vice and misery are products of corrupt institutions, not innate human depravity. Once tyranny (political, religious, domestic) is removed, human beings will become progressively more rational, moral, and happy. The philosophical foundation of Shelley's political optimism.

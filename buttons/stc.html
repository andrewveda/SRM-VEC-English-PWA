Samuel Taylor Coleridge

1772 — 1834

"Water, water, every where, / Nor any drop to drink."

I

Life & Biography

Origins — Devon clergyman's son
Born 21 October 1772 at Ottery St Mary, Devon — the youngest of ten children of the Revd John Coleridge, vicar and schoolmaster. An intensely bookish, precocious child; his father died when he was nine. His older brothers resented his cleverness; his father had clearly favoured him. Sent to Christ's Hospital, London (1782) — the famous "Blue Coat" charity school — where he encountered Charles Lamb and was taught by the inspiring James Bowyer, who drilled him in classical rhetoric and showed him how bad writing fails. These formative years in an institution, away from family, mark the beginning of Coleridge's characteristic double life: brilliant in public, deeply lonely in private.
Cambridge & radicalism — Pantisocracy
Entered Jesus College, Cambridge (1791). Brilliant but chaotic — left debts unpaid, enlisted in the 15th Light Dragoons under the alias "Silas Tomkyn Comberbache" (discharged as "insane" after a few months), and returned to Cambridge. Met Robert Southey in 1794; together they conceived Pantisocracy — a plan to establish a utopian egalitarian community on the banks of the Susquehanna River in Pennsylvania, modelled on Godwinian ideals. It never happened (Southey lost interest and Coleridge lacked money) but it produced his first significant poems and his ill-fated engagement to Sara Fricker (Southey's sister-in-law), whom he married in 1795 despite being in love with someone else.
The Wordsworth years (1797–1802) — the great burst
Coleridge met William Wordsworth in 1795; by 1797 they were neighbours in Somerset (Coleridge at Nether Stowey, the Wordsworths at Alfoxton). The eighteen months that followed produced the greatest concentrated outpouring of Coleridge's creative life: The Rime of the Ancient Mariner (1797–98), Kubla Khan (composition disputed: 1797 or 1799), Christabel Part I (1797), and the Conversation Poems. The collaboration with Wordsworth produced Lyrical Ballads (1798) — the foundational text of English Romanticism. The terms of the collaboration: Wordsworth would handle "incidents and agents… naturally human"; Coleridge would handle "persons and characters supernatural… yet so as to transfer from our inward nature a human interest and a semblance of truth."
Opium — the defining catastrophe
Coleridge first took laudanum (opium dissolved in alcohol) around 1796 for the treatment of rheumatic pain. By 1800 he was addicted. Opium dependency shaped everything in the second half of his life: the failure to complete Christabel and Kubla Khan; the creative paralysis documented in Dejection: An Ode; the quarrel with Wordsworth; the disintegration of his marriage; the years of dependency on others. He eventually placed himself under the care of Dr James Gillman in Highgate (1816) and lived there, relatively stable, for the last eighteen years of his life. The Highgate years produced the Biographia Literaria, the literary lectures, and the Table Talk. The question of how much opium shaped or inhibited his poetry remains central to Coleridge scholarship.
Sara Hutchinson & Asra
Coleridge fell deeply in love with Sara Hutchinson (Wordsworth's future sister-in-law) around 1799. He was married to Sara Fricker; Sara Hutchinson was attached to Wordsworth's circle. The unrequited passion — he called her "Asra" in his poetry — lasted for years and produced Dejection: An Ode (originally a verse letter addressed directly to Sara Hutchinson). The relationship illuminates the painful disjunction in Coleridge's life between his philosophical ideals and his emotional reality. He copied out thousands of words in his notebooks about her. She eventually retreated from his intensity.
The quarrel with Wordsworth (1810)
In 1810, Coleridge discovered that Wordsworth had told Basil Montagu — who was offering Coleridge lodgings — that Coleridge was "a rotten drunkard" who had "lost himself entirely" and who would be a burden. Wordsworth's words, reported back to Coleridge, shattered their friendship. They were partly reconciled but never recovered the intimacy of the Stowey years. The quarrel is important for understanding the Biographia Literaria (1817) — which contains both the most generous assessment of Wordsworth's genius in the language and a sustained, cutting critique of his theoretical failings.
Later life — the Highgate sage
From 1816 until his death in 1834, Coleridge lived at Moreton House, Highgate, with the Gillman family. He held "Thursday evenings" at which younger writers came to listen — Carlyle, Hazlitt, Emerson, John Stuart Mill, and many others recorded the experience of hearing Coleridge talk (often for hours without pause). His conversation was legendary — Keats heard him discourse on nightingales and a dozen other topics in a single walk; Hazlitt called it "the first emanation of genius I had ever seen." He died 25 July 1834, aged 61, of heart failure complicated by lung disease.

NET trap: Coleridge's three "supernatural" masterpieces were composed c.1797–1800 but published at very different times. The Rime of the Ancient Mariner appeared in Lyrical Ballads (1798). Christabel and Kubla Khan were published together in 1816 — nearly two decades after composition. This gap between composition and publication is one of the most tested bibliographic facts in NET.
II

The Three Supernatural Poems

The Rime of the Ancient Mariner NET HIGH
Composed 1797–98 · Published Lyrical Ballads 1798 · Revised 1800, 1817 · 625 lines
▼
The ballad stanza
It is an ancient Mariner, And he stoppeth one of three. 'By thy long grey beard and glittering eye, Now wherefore stopp'st thou me? The Bridegroom's doors are opened wide, And I am next of kin; The guests are met, the feast is set: May'st hear the merry din.' He holds him with his skinny hand, 'There was a ship,' quoth he. 'Hold off! unhand me, grey-beard loon!' Eftsoons his hand dropt he.
Plot summary
A Wedding Guest is stopped outside a wedding feast by an old sailor. The Mariner tells his story compulsively: his ship was driven south by storms to the Antarctic; an albatross appeared through the fog and was welcomed as a Christian soul; the Mariner shot the albatross with his crossbow (no reason given). The crew first condemned him, then — when the fog lifted — praised him. The ship sailed into the "silent sea" and was becalmed. Water everywhere but nothing to drink. The crew hung the dead albatross around the Mariner's neck as penance. The crew died one by one; the Mariner alone survived. A skeleton ship appeared with two figures — Death and Life-in-Death — who diced for the Mariner's soul; Life-in-Death won (a worse fate). The Mariner watched his crew die; he was unable even to pray. He looked at the water-snakes in moonlight and "a spring of love gushed from my heart" — he blessed them "unaware." The albatross fell from his neck. Rain came. The ship moved by supernatural power (the crew animated by spirits). An angelic voice gave the Mariner partial absolution; a hermit would shrive him. He returned home; the ship sank; the hermit shrived him but could not fully release him — he is condemned to wander, telling his story to those who need to hear it. The moral: "He prayeth best, who loveth best / All things both great and small."
Structural features
Ballad form: The poem imitates medieval ballads — quatrains of alternating iambic tetrameter and trimeter, ballad conventions (repetition, direct speech, incremental repetition). But Coleridge extends the ballad stanza with additional lines, inserts archaic diction ("eftsoons," "I wist"), and adds the marginal glosses in the 1817 edition.

The marginal gloss (added 1817): A prose commentary running alongside the poem — written in a different, cooler, more rational voice than the poem itself. The gloss interprets and explains events that the poem leaves mysterious. Crucially, the gloss and the poem sometimes diverge — what the gloss says happened and what the poem presents are not always identical. This creates a double structure: the poem's irrational/supernatural experience vs. the gloss's rationalising commentary.

Frame narrative: The Mariner's tale is embedded in the frame of the Wedding Guest — who is stopped, compelled, and transformed (he wakes "a sadder and a wiser man"). The frame raises questions about the purpose of the tale and the Mariner's compulsion to tell it.
Critical debates — the six most important
1. Why does the Mariner shoot the albatross?
The poem gives no reason — the act is "motiveless" (like Iago's malignity). The absence of motive is the point: the poem is about the consequences of an act performed without understanding, not about a deliberate wrong. This connects to the theme of will, crime, and redemption.

2. The moral — is it adequate?
"He prayeth best, who loveth best / All things both great and small." Mrs Barbauld complained there was no moral; Coleridge replied the poem had "too much." The moral seems disproportionately simple for the cosmic journey it concludes. Critics debate whether this simplicity is a failure or a deliberately inadequate resolution — the Mariner's spiritual understanding remains limited even after suffering.

3. The supernatural vs. the natural:
Wordsworth designed the supernatural Lyrical Ballads poems to transfer "a human interest and semblance of truth" — to make the supernatural psychologically credible. Does the Mariner achieve this? The 1800 revision removed some of the more archaic touches Wordsworth found excessive. Wordsworth's note in later editions distanced him from the poem — he thought it had too many "stoppages," that the Mariner was too passive, and that the moral was "awkward."

4. The crime and redemption structure:
Robert Penn Warren's influential reading: the poem has two levels — the surface (crime of shooting the albatross → punishment → partial redemption) and the deeper symbolic level (the crime of separating oneself from community with nature and mankind → reconnection through love). The water-snakes blessing is the central act of reconnection.

5. Psychological / opium reading:
Some critics read the Mariner's experience as a projection of Coleridge's own guilt and addiction — the compulsive telling as a figure for the addict's compulsive use; the Life-in-Death as the waking hell of addiction; the partial redemption as his own inability to fully escape.

6. Postcolonial reading:
The voyage to the Antarctic and the colonial trade routes underlying the poem; the Mariner's encounter with otherness (the albatross as colonial other?). Alan Bewell and others have explored the poem's relationship to imperial geography.
Key quotations for NET
"Water, water, every where, / Nor any drop to drink" (Part II) — commonly misquoted as "but not a drop to drink"

Publication history — critical for NET
First published: Lyrical Ballads (September 1798) — anonymously. Revised for Lyrical Ballads (1800) — Coleridge modernised some archaic spellings, removed some lines Wordsworth disliked. Further revised with the marginal glosses added for Sibylline Leaves (1817). The 1817 text, with the glosses, is the standard scholarly text. Three significant versions exist — a NET examination point.
Kubla Khan NET HIGH
Composition disputed: 1797 or 1799 · Published 1816 · 54 lines
▼
The poem
In Xanadu did Kubla Khan A stately pleasure-dome decree: Where Alph, the sacred river, ran Through caverns measureless to man Down to a sunless sea. So twice five miles of fertile ground With walls and towers were girdled round; And there were gardens bright with sinuous rills, Where blossomed many an incense-bearing tree; And here were forests ancient as the hills, Enfolding sunny spots of greenery.
Coleridge's preface — the Person from Porlock
Coleridge prefaced the 1816 publication with an account of the poem's composition: he had fallen asleep (after taking "an anodyne" — opium) while reading Purchas's Pilgrimage, the passage about "Kubla Khan's palace." He "continued for about three hours in a profound sleep" during which the poem composed itself — "without any sensation or consciousness of effort." He awoke and began writing it down, but was then interrupted by "a person on business from Porlock" for "above an hour." When he returned, the vision had dispersed — he could recover only "eight or ten scattered lines." The poem is therefore a fragment, not a complete work.

The reliability of this account is extensively debated. Elizabeth Schneider argued the poem was carefully revised over time — not a spontaneous dream-composition. John Livingston Lowes (The Road to Xanadu, 1927) traced almost every image in the poem to books Coleridge had read — suggesting a composed, not dreamed, origin. The preface may be a literary fiction (the "fragment" as genre; the "dream-vision" as convention). Nevertheless, the Person from Porlock has become one of the most famous figures in literary mythology — standing for the creative interruption, the destroyed vision, the gap between imagination and execution.
Structure & themes
The poem divides into three parts:
Lines 1–36: Description of Xanadu — the pleasure-dome, the sacred river Alph, the caverns, the "deep romantic chasm," the "demon-lover," the ancestral voices prophesying war.
Lines 37–41: The speaker shifts — "A damsel with a dulcimer / In a vision once I saw." Now the poet is not describing Xanadu but recalling a vision of a woman who could inspire him to recreate it.
Lines 42–54: The conditional subjunctive — "Could I revive within me / Her symphony and song, / To such a deep delight 'twould win me, / That with music loud and long, / I would build that dome in air." The poem is about the impossibility of building the dome — the failure of imagination to complete what inspiration began.

The pleasure-dome vs. the chasm: Kubla Khan's dome (human order, civilisation, art) is built over/beside the "deep romantic chasm" from which the sacred river erupts with violent creativity. Order and chaos, civilisation and the unconscious, the made and the unmade are in constant tension. The dome cannot contain the chasm; the caverns and the sunless sea wait beneath.

The Abyssinian maid: In the final section, the poet figure who could rebuild the dome in imagination would frighten onlookers — "his flashing eyes, his floating hair" — who would recognise him as one possessed by the demonic creative force: "Weave a circle round him thrice, / And close your eyes with holy dread."
Critical readings
Humphry House: The poem is about the creative process itself — the dome is the work of art; the chasm is inspiration; the failure to complete it is the Romantic condition.
John Livingston Lowes (The Road to Xanadu): The poem's imagery is systematically traceable to Coleridge's reading — Purchas, Bruce's Travels to Discover the Source of the Nile, Bartram's Travels. The "creative" process is one of unconscious recombination.
Harold Bloom: The poem is Coleridge's most direct statement of his failure — the inability to sustain visionary creation, figured as the incomplete dome.
Psychoanalytic: The sacred river erupting from the chasm, flowing through caverns, and sinking into the sea: an obvious sexual topography; the poem's erotic charge connects to opium-induced fantasy.
NET trap: Kubla Khan was composed c.1797 (or possibly 1799 — debated) but published in 1816. The 1816 volume also contained Christabel and The Pains of Sleep. It was published at Byron's urging — Byron had heard Coleridge recite it. The date of composition vs. publication gap is a consistent NET question.
Christabel Unfinished
Part I: 1797 · Part II: 1800 · Published 1816 · Fragment: 677 lines
▼
Plot & atmosphere
Part I: Christabel, daughter of Sir Leoline, goes alone at midnight to pray under an oak tree. She finds there a beautiful woman, Geraldine, who claims to have been abducted. Christabel brings her home. Christabel's mother's spirit (protective) is present. When Geraldine passes the threshold she stumbles — a witch cannot enter unless invited. Geraldine undresses, revealing something hideous on her side (never explicitly described). She lies with Christabel, claiming a night of possession. In the morning Christabel is altered — "for this one night" she is possessed by Geraldine's "sight."

Part II: Sir Leoline receives Geraldine, who claims to be the daughter of his estranged friend Lord Roland. Sir Leoline is moved — he resolves to restore her. Christabel tries to warn her father but can only express herself with "a hiss" — she has been contaminated by Geraldine's serpent nature. Sir Leoline dismisses her. The poem breaks off before the promised confrontation.
The verse form — Coleridge's innovation
Coleridge invented what he called "a new principle" of versification: each line has four accented syllables, but the number of unaccented syllables varies freely. This is accentual verse (as opposed to the syllabic iambic pentameter). This principle was adopted and acknowledged by Scott (in The Lay of the Last Minstrel, 1805) and Byron (in The Siege of Corinth, 1816) — both before Christabel was published. Scott's and Byron's use of the "Christabel metre" spread it through 19th-century poetry even before the poem appeared in print.
Critical themes
• The vampire reading: Geraldine is a lamia/vampire figure — she feeds on Christabel's innocence. The poem is a precursor to the Gothic vampire tradition that culminates in Polidori's The Vampyre (1819) and Sheridan Le Fanu's Carmilla (1872).
• The lesbian reading: The bed-scene between Christabel and Geraldine has been read as a representation of sexual transgression between women — both a suppressed homoerotic desire and an anxiety about female sexuality.
• The innocence/experience theme: Christabel's innocence is corrupted by contact with Geraldine's experience — parallel to Blake's Songs. The father's failure to protect her mirrors Coleridge's anxieties about male authority.
• Why unfinished? Coleridge told Gillman and others what he intended for Parts III, IV, and V — Christabel was to become Geraldine-like herself; there would be a final redemption. Whether he simply could not write it (addiction, depression, self-doubt) or whether the poem's logic defeated completion is a critical question.
III

The Conversation Poems

What are the Conversation Poems?
A group of meditative blank-verse lyrics named by critic George McLean Harper (1928) — Coleridge himself did not use the term. The poems all share: a domestic or local setting; a speaker meditating in solitude or near a sleeping companion; a movement outward from personal feeling to a natural scene; a return inward with a modified or deepened understanding. The major examples: The Eolian Harp (1795), This Lime-Tree Bower My Prison (1797), Frost at Midnight (1798), Fears in Solitude (1798), Dejection: An Ode (1802). They represent Coleridge's most purely personal voice — and they directly influenced Wordsworth's meditative lyrics, including Tintern Abbey.
Frost at Midnight NET HIGH
February 1798 · Published 1798 · 74 lines · Blank verse
▼
The poem
The Frost performs its secret ministry, Unhelped by any wind. The owlet's cry Came loud — and hark, again! loud as before. The inmates of my cottage, all at rest, Have left me to that solitude, which suits Abstruser musings: save that at my side My cradled infant slumbers peacefully.
Structure & movement
The poem moves through three phases:
1. The midnight scene: The poet sits alone by the fire; his infant son (Hartley) sleeps in the cradle. A "fluttering stranger" (soot on the grate, believed by superstition to presage the arrival of a friend) attracts his attention. He meditates on the silence.
2. Memory: The stranger sends him back to his own childhood at Christ's Hospital — he would watch the same film of soot on the grate and dream of returning home to Ottery St Mary. He longed for his home, his birthplace, the natural world he had been separated from.
3. The turn to the child: He addresses baby Hartley — his son will have what he lacked: he will grow up in the natural world, "wander like a breeze / By lakes and sandy shores." Nature will speak to him "in all seasons" — the great Romantic promise of a child educated by nature rather than by institution.
4. Return to the frost: The poem closes as it opened, with the frost — but now enriched. The frost's "secret ministry" of forming icicles is now understood as analogous to the poem's own work of making meaning in silence and darkness.
Critical significance
Frost at Midnight is the finest of the Conversation Poems and one of the most perfect meditative lyrics in English. Its structure — circular, returning to the opening image but transformed — became the model for Wordsworth's Tintern Abbey (written five months later). The child Hartley Coleridge becomes a figure of the Romantic hope for natural education — the same hope that drives Book I of The Prelude. The poem is also a confession: Coleridge had been denied this natural childhood; the poem is simultaneously a gift to his son and an elegy for himself.
Dejection: An Ode NET HIGH
1802 · Originally a verse letter to Sara Hutchinson · Published Morning Post 1802
▼
Origin — the verse letter
Dejection was originally composed as a 340-line verse letter to Sara Hutchinson (the woman Coleridge loved but could not have), written on 4 April 1802 — the morning after Wordsworth had read the first four stanzas of the Intimations of Immortality ode. The verse letter is raw, personal, confessional — it names Sara directly, details Coleridge's suffering from his marriage, his addiction, his creative paralysis. For publication in the Morning Post (4 October 1802 — Wordsworth's wedding day), Coleridge revised and shortened it, replacing "Sara" with "Lady," removing the most personal confessions, and shaping it into the formal ode that is now the standard text. The full verse letter was not published until the 20th century.
Key stanza — the loss of imagination
There was a time when, though my path was rough, This joy within me dallied with distress, And all misfortunes were but as the stuff Whence Fancy made me dreams of happiness: For hope grew round me, like the twining vine, And fruits, and foliage, not my own, seemed mine. But now afflictions bow me down to earth: Nor care I that they rob me of my mirth; But oh! each visitation Suspends what nature gave me at my birth, My shaping spirit of Imagination.
The central argument
The poem's central claim is that the imagination does not receive beauty from nature — it projects it: "I may not hope from outward forms to win / The passion and the life, whose fountains are within." We receive from nature only what we bring to it. When the "shaping spirit of Imagination" fails — as Coleridge's has, through depression, addiction, and grief — nature becomes merely "reality's dark dream."

This is philosophically important: it refutes (or at least complicates) the Wordsworthian claim that nature has inherent healing power. For Coleridge, the power is always already within us — and when it fails, no amount of nature can restore it. The poem is simultaneously a confession of creative failure and a sophisticated philosophical statement about the imagination's priority over perception.
Relationship to Wordsworth's Immortality Ode
Dejection and the Immortality Ode were written in close dialogue — Wordsworth on the same April morning had been working on "There was a time when meadow, grove, and stream…" Both poems address the loss of visionary power; both ask whether it can be recovered. But they give different answers: Wordsworth finds consolation in "years that bring the philosophic mind"; Coleridge finds no such consolation — the "shaping spirit of Imagination" is simply lost. The two poems are mirror-texts of Romantic crisis.
The Eolian Harp
1795 · Published 1796 · Blank verse
▼
Significance
The first and most philosophically ambitious of the Conversation Poems. A harp in a window makes music as the breeze passes through it — the Aeolian harp image (a natural instrument played by wind) becomes a metaphor for the mind's relationship to the natural world. The poem's central, famous speculation: "And what if all of animated nature / Be but organic Harps diversely framed, / That tremble into thought, as o'er them sweeps / Plastic and vast, one intellectual Breeze, / At once the Soul of each, and God of all?" — a pantheist, Spinozan vision. But the poem then retreats: his wife Sara gives him "a mild reproof" and he recants, calling the speculation "shapings of the unregenerate mind." The self-censorship within the poem is its most honest moment — Coleridge's philosophical boldness was always in tension with his orthodox Christian commitments.
This Lime-Tree Bower My Prison
1797 · Published 1800 · Blank verse
▼
Context & argument
Written when Coleridge was left behind at Nether Stowey while Wordsworth, Dorothy Wordsworth, and Charles Lamb walked to the Quantock Hills — he had scalded his foot (his wife Sara had accidentally spilled boiling milk). Confined to a lime-tree bower in the garden, he imaginatively accompanies his friends on their walk, describes what they will see, and arrives at the conclusion that no place is without beauty if the mind attends: "No plot so narrow, be but Nature there, / No waste so vacant, but may well employ / Each faculty of sense, and keep the heart / Awake to Love and Beauty!" The poem demonstrates the "One Life" philosophy: all nature is connected, all places can be loci of transcendence.
IV

Other Notable Poems

The Pains of Sleep
1803 · Published 1816 (with Kubla Khan & Christabel)
▼
Significance
A frank account of opium-withdrawal nightmares — the poem describes three successive nights of tortured dreams, shame, and self-horror. "But yester-night I prayed aloud / In anguish and in agony." Published alongside Kubla Khan (the dream-vision) in 1816, The Pains of Sleep reads as its dark inversion — if Kubla Khan is the ecstatic dream, The Pains of Sleep is the nightmarish reality of addiction. The three poems together (Christabel, Kubla Khan, The Pains of Sleep) form a coherent 1816 volume exploring the supernatural, visionary creation, and its psychological cost.
Work Without Hope
February 1825 · Highgate years
▼
All Nature seems at work. Slugs leave their lair — The bees are stirring — birds are on the wing — And Winter slumbering in the open air, Wears on his smiling face a dream of Spring! And I the while, the sole unbusy thing, Nor honey make, nor pair, nor build, nor sing. Yet well I know the banks where amaranths blow, Have traced the fount whence streams of nectar flow. Bloom, O ye amaranths! bloom for whom ye may, For me ye bloom not! Glide, rich streams, away! With lips unbrightened, wreathless brow, I stroll: And would you learn the spells that drowse my soul? Work without Hope draws nectar in a sieve, And Hope without an object cannot live.
Significance
Written from Highgate in 1825, this is one of Coleridge's most poignant late poems — the contrast between nature's busy renewal and the poet's paralysis. All creatures work; Coleridge alone is "the sole unbusy thing." The concluding couplet — "Work without Hope draws nectar in a sieve, / And Hope without an object cannot live" — is a perfectly turned expression of his creative impasse: he can see the nectar (beauty, truth, poetry) but cannot hold it.
Fears in Solitude & France: An Ode
1798 · Political poems
▼
Significance
Two important political poems of 1798, written during the threat of French invasion. Fears in Solitude is a meditation on England's moral failure — the slave trade, shallow churchgoing, warmongering rhetoric — that has left it vulnerable to the punishment of invasion. France: An Ode marks the moment of Coleridge's political disillusionment with the French Revolution — specifically, the French invasion of Switzerland (1798) proves that France had betrayed the cause of liberty it claimed to represent. Both poems document the transition from the revolutionary optimism of 1793–95 to the political conservatism of the later Coleridge.
V

Prose Works

Biographia Literaria (1817) NET HIGH
Published 1817 · 2 volumes · Autobiography, literary criticism, philosophy
▼
What it is
Nominally an autobiography, the Biographia Literaria is the most important work of literary criticism produced in the English Romantic period. It is digressive, unfinished, partly plagiarised (the philosophy is heavily drawn from Schelling without acknowledgement), and yet contains passages of extraordinary acuity and insight. Its structure: Chapters I–IV discuss Coleridge's early literary development and the influence of Bowyer. Chapters V–XIII work through German Idealist philosophy (Fichte, Schelling, Kant) toward a theory of imagination. Chapters XIV–XXII contain the critique of Wordsworth — both the most generous tribute to Wordsworth's genius and the most penetrating analysis of his failures.
The distinction between Primary and Secondary Imagination
Chapter XIII contains the most famous passage in English Romantic criticism:

Primary Imagination: "the living Power and prime Agent of all human Perception, and as a repetition in the finite mind of the eternal act of creation in the infinite I AM." — The primary imagination is the unconscious, universal creative faculty through which all perception operates. It is what makes the world appear as a unified whole rather than disconnected sense-data. Every human being uses it simply by perceiving.

Secondary Imagination: "an echo of the former, co-existing with the conscious will, yet still as identical with the primary in the kind of its agency, and differing only in degree, and in the mode of its operation. It dissolves, diffuses, dissipates, in order to recreate; or where this process is rendered impossible, yet still at all events it struggles to idealise and to unify. It is essentially vital, even as all objects (as objects) are essentially fixed and dead." — The secondary imagination is the conscious creative faculty of the poet. It does not create from nothing — it breaks down existing perceptions and recombines them into new, living wholes. It is a conscious, effortful echo of the primary imagination's unconscious power.

Fancy: "no other than a mode of Memory emancipated from the order of time and space; while it is blended with, and modified by that empirical phenomenon of the will, which we express by the word CHOICE. But equally with the ordinary memory the Fancy must receive all its materials ready made from the law of association." — Fancy is associative, mechanical, combinatorial. It takes existing elements and rearranges them; it does not transform them. The distinction between Imagination (which transforms) and Fancy (which merely recombines) is one of the central concepts of Romantic aesthetics. It follows from a distinction Coleridge found in Kant and Schelling but reworked for English poetics.
The critique of Wordsworth (Chapters XVII–XXII)
Coleridge identifies the chief defect in Wordsworth's poetry as "INCONSTANCY OF THE STYLE" — moments of "sudden and unexpected" sinking from high imaginative verse into flat prosaic matter-of-fact language. He also challenges Wordsworth's Preface to Lyrical Ballads claim that the language of low and rustic life is inherently purer and more philosophical than educated language: "the best part of human language, properly so called, is derived from reflection on the acts of the mind itself."

But Coleridge's praise is equally remarkable: he identifies Wordsworth's five chief excellences — the "fine balance of truth in observing, with the imaginative faculty in modifying, the objects observed" — and declares that a long poem with consistently high imagination "is not possible" before identifying The Prelude as "the first genuine philosophic poem."
The plagiarism problem
Thomas De Quincey first publicly identified extensive plagiarism from Schelling's System des transcendentalen Idealismus in the Biographia. Long passages are translated almost verbatim without acknowledgement. Modern scholars debate whether this was deliberate deception, the result of opium-addled memory (he may have thought the ideas his own), or a 19th-century concept of intellectual "absorption" that was not plagiarism in the modern sense. The issue does not reduce the importance of the work — but it complicates reading it as purely original thought.
Lectures on Shakespeare & Literary Criticism
1808, 1811–12, 1813, 1818–19 · Royal Institution & other venues
▼
Significance
Coleridge's Shakespeare lectures were not published in his lifetime — they survive through shorthand notes taken by audience members and through Coleridge's own preparation notes. They represent the first major treatment of Shakespeare as a conscious, deliberate artist — overturning the Neoclassical view (Voltaire, earlier critics) that Shakespeare was an "inspired barbarian" who succeeded through instinct and vitality rather than art.

His most famous critical act: the reading of Hamlet. Coleridge argued that Hamlet's delay is caused by "an over-balance of the contemplative faculty over the active" — excess of intellect paralyses will. He famously added: "I have a smack of Hamlet myself, if I may say so." This self-identification is one of the most candid moments in criticism — but it is also, critics note, a projection: Coleridge reads Hamlet through his own paralysis and addiction. The reading is both illuminating and self-serving.

Coleridge also argued against the "mechanical regularity" of French neoclassical criticism and for an "organic form" in Shakespeare — the play growing from within according to its own internal necessity rather than following external rules. This distinction (mechanical vs. organic) is central to Romantic aesthetics.
Aids to Reflection & The Friend
The Friend (1809–10, revised 1818) · Aids to Reflection (1825)
▼
Significance
The Friend was a periodical Coleridge produced (with enormous effort, assisted by Sara Hutchinson) between 1809 and 1810 — it ran to 28 issues before collapsing. It contained essays on politics, philosophy, and literature. Revised as a book in 1818, it contains some of Coleridge's most substantial political philosophy.

Aids to Reflection (1825) was enormously influential on 19th-century religious thought — particularly in America, where it shaped Transcendentalism (Emerson acknowledged his debt) and later liberal Protestant theology. It distinguishes "Reason" (the intuitive, spiritual faculty) from "Understanding" (the analytical, empirical faculty) — a distinction that runs through all his work.
Table Talk
Recorded conversations from the Highgate years — published posthumously (1836) by his nephew Henry Nelson Coleridge. The Table Talk preserves Coleridge's spoken criticism and philosophy — often more vivid and direct than his written work. Contains some of his most quoted remarks: on Hamlet, on Shakespeare, on the distinction between talent and genius, on the nature of poetry. A primary source for Romantic critical thought.
VI

Key Philosophical Concepts

Primary vs Secondary Imagination vs Fancy Most tested
Biographia Literaria, Chapter XIII
▼
The definitions — memorise these
Primary Imagination: The unconscious, universal creative faculty of all human perception. "The living Power and prime Agent of all human Perception." A repetition in the finite human mind of the infinite act of divine creation. Every person uses it — it is not uniquely poetic; it is the foundation of all conscious experience.

Secondary Imagination: The conscious, voluntary creative power of the poet. An "echo" of the primary imagination — "co-existing with the conscious will." It "dissolves, diffuses, dissipates, in order to recreate." It takes the world apart and puts it back together in new, living forms. It is "essentially vital" — it gives life to what was dead.

Fancy: A lesser faculty — merely associative. "A mode of Memory emancipated from the order of time and space." Fancy takes existing material and rearranges it by the law of association; it does not transform. A clever simile is Fancy; a metaphor that reveals a hidden truth is Imagination.
Why this distinction matters
The Imagination/Fancy distinction gives Coleridge the critical vocabulary to distinguish great poetry from merely clever poetry. Milton's Satan is Imagination; a witty conceit by Cowley is Fancy. The distinction also separates Coleridge from Hartley's associationist psychology (which could only explain Fancy) and aligns him with Kant and Schelling's idealist philosophy. It is the theoretical underpinning of all his literary criticism.
The Willing Suspension of Disbelief NET HIGH
Biographia Literaria, Chapter XIV
▼
The concept

"...it was agreed, that my endeavours should be directed to persons and characters supernatural, or at least romantic, yet so as to transfer from our inward nature a human interest and a semblance of truth sufficient to procure for these shadows of imagination that willing suspension of disbelief for the moment, which constitutes poetic faith."

— Biographia Literaria, Chapter XIV
The "willing suspension of disbelief" is Coleridge's description of how supernatural poetry works on a reader. The reader does not truly believe the events — they know it is fiction — but they willingly set aside their disbelief in order to experience the work's emotional and imaginative effects. This is "poetic faith" — not the same as religious faith, but analogous. The phrase has become one of the most widely used concepts in literary and cultural theory, applied far beyond its original context of supernatural poetry to all fiction, theatre, film, and virtual reality.
The division of labour with Wordsworth
Coleridge explains that the conception of Lyrical Ballads arose from a division: Wordsworth would make "incidents from common life" seem strange and interesting; Coleridge would make "supernatural or romantic" events seem humanly credible. The willing suspension of disbelief is specifically Coleridge's half of the bargain — the reader agrees to inhabit the supernatural world of the Ancient Mariner as if it were real.
Organic vs Mechanical Form NET HIGH
Shakespeare lectures; "On Poesy or Art"
▼
The distinction
Mechanical form: Form imposed from outside — a pre-existing mould into which content is poured. Like a stone shaped by a sculptor from without. The neoclassical unities (time, place, action) are mechanical rules — imposed on the play from classical authority rather than arising from the play's own nature.

Organic form: Form that grows from within — inseparable from content. Like a plant that develops its shape from its own internal principle. Shakespeare's plays have organic form — each play determines its own structure. The apparent "irregularities" (mixing comic and tragic, ignoring the unities) are not failures but the expression of the play's own necessity.

Coleridge derived this from A.W. Schlegel (Lectures on Dramatic Art and Literature, 1808), which he heard and read in Germany. As with Schelling and the imagination, the debt is often under-acknowledged.
Reason vs Understanding
Aids to Reflection; The Friend
▼
The distinction
Derived from Kant's distinction between Vernunft and Verstand:

Understanding: The faculty of logical, analytical, empirical thought — breaks things into parts, categorises, measures. The tool of science and everyday reasoning. Has access only to the material world.

Reason: The higher faculty of intuitive, spiritual insight — apprehends wholes rather than parts; apprehends moral and spiritual truths. Has access to the supersensible. In Aids to Reflection, Reason is associated with faith, conscience, and spiritual perception.

This distinction was enormously influential on Emerson and American Transcendentalism — Emerson took it from Coleridge and made it the foundation of his distinction between the materialist "Understanding" and the idealist "Reason" in Nature (1836).
The "One Life" philosophy
Running through the Conversation Poems — especially The Eolian Harp and This Lime-Tree Bower — is what Coleridge called the "One Life" philosophy: the idea that all living things are animated by the same divine spirit; that nature is not dead mechanism but a living whole in which humanity participates. This pantheist tendency was always in tension with his orthodox Christian commitments — and the self-censorship visible in The Eolian Harp (where Sara's reproving look causes him to recant the pantheist speculation) is the tension made visible.
VII

Major Themes

The supernatural & psychological depth
Coleridge's supernatural is not simply Gothic atmosphere — it is the externalisation of psychological states. The Mariner's guilt and isolation are projected into the supernatural environment: the becalmed sea, the skeleton ship, the possessed crew. The supernatural events in Christabel (Geraldine's hideous body, the serpent-contamination) are expressions of psychological violation. This is why the "willing suspension of disbelief" works — the supernatural is not arbitrary but psychologically coherent. It speaks from the "inward nature" of the reader.
Creative failure, guilt & the imagination
Coleridge's most persistent personal theme is the failure of the imagination — not as a philosophical abstraction but as a lived experience. Dejection: An Ode is its most direct expression. The unfinished state of Christabel and Kubla Khan; the Biographia Literaria's account of never completing the great philosophical poem he planned; the opium-controlled paralysis — all circle this theme. Coleridge turned creative failure into a subject of poetry and criticism more penetratingly than any other Romantic writer.
Crime, guilt & the need for community
The Mariner's crime separates him from human community — he can only return through the act of love (blessing the water-snakes). The poem is structured by the movement from isolation (the lone shooter) through suffering to partial reintegration (the compulsive storytelling that connects him to the Wedding Guest). Christabel's contamination also isolates her from her father. For Coleridge, the primary human condition is relational — crime and sin are acts of self-isolation; redemption is the restoration of connection.
Nature as spiritual medium
In the Conversation Poems, nature is not merely scenery — it is a medium through which the "One Life" expresses itself. The frost's "secret ministry," the lime-tree bower's unexpected richness, the Aeolian harp's music — all are instances of nature speaking the divine. But Coleridge, unlike Wordsworth, is uncertain whether nature has this power inherently or only through the mind's projection. Dejection resolves the question pessimistically: nature is what the mind makes it. When the mind fails, nature is silent.
Childhood, education & the natural self
Frost at Midnight is organised around the contrast between Coleridge's institutional childhood (Christ's Hospital, removed from nature) and the natural childhood he promises Hartley. This is the Rousseauian and Wordsworthian ideal — the child educated by nature. But Coleridge's is always a wistful gift: he knows what he lacked; he does not know whether Hartley will receive it. The poem is simultaneously hopeful and elegiac.
VIII

Critical Approaches

I.A. Richards (1926) Coleridge on Imagination — the first sustained philosophical analysis of the imagination/fancy distinction. Richards took Coleridge seriously as a systematic thinker and rehabilitated him after Victorian neglect. Essential for New Criticism's engagement with Coleridge.
John Livingston Lowes (1927) The Road to Xanadu — traced the sources of The Ancient Mariner and Kubla Khan in Coleridge's reading notebooks. Demonstrated the creative process as unconscious synthesis. Enormously influential; also partially undermines the "spontaneous composition" claims.
Robert Penn Warren (1946) "A Poem of Pure Imagination" — argued The Ancient Mariner has two structural levels: the surface (crime and punishment) and the deeper symbolic level (the theme of the "One Life" — love and imagination). The water-snakes blessing is the poem's pivot: the moment when the Mariner reconnects with the One Life.
Elisabeth Schneider (1953) Coleridge, Opium and Kubla Khan — challenged the spontaneous dream-composition account of Kubla Khan. Argued the poem was carefully composed over time; the "Person from Porlock" story may be fictional. Placed Coleridge's opium in the context of the medical understanding of the period.
Thomas McFarland (1969) Coleridge and the Pantheist Tradition — placed Coleridge's philosophical work in the context of Spinoza, Leibniz, and German Idealism. Argued Coleridge's "plagiarism" of Schelling was a form of philosophical absorption — he metabolised rather than stole.
Jerome McGann (New Historicism) Read Coleridge's political conservatism (especially in the later work) as a suppression of the radical political implications of his early thought. The Biographia's aestheticising move — making literature autonomous from politics — is itself a political act.
Feminist criticism Anne Mellor, Marlon Ross. Examined the construction of gender in the supernatural poems: Geraldine as a figure of dangerous female sexuality; Sara Hutchinson's "reproving look" in The Eolian Harp as feminine authority constraining male speculation. Also: Mary Wollstonecraft's critique of sensibility is relevant to Coleridge's emotional culture.
Psychoanalytic Humphry House, Beverly Fields. The opium-shaped poems read as expressions of addiction psychology: the compulsive return (the Mariner's compulsive telling), the threshold-crossing (Christabel's night), the lost vision (Kubla Khan). Coleridge as the first Romantic poet to make psychological crisis the explicit subject of major poetry.
IX

Literary & Historical Context

Lyrical Ballads (1798) — the founding text
Published jointly by Wordsworth and Coleridge in September 1798. Wordsworth contributed the majority of poems; Coleridge contributed The Rime of the Ancient Mariner, The Nightingale, The Foster-Mother's Tale, and The Dungeon. The Advertisement to the 1798 edition said the poems were "experiments" to discover how far "natural language" could give pleasure. The 1800 second edition expanded Wordsworth's contribution and added the famous Preface — Coleridge did not write it but helped shape it. The key terms: "poetry of ordinary experience" vs. "poetry of the supernatural"; "spontaneous overflow of powerful feelings"; "language really used by men." Lyrical Ballads is the founding text of English literary Romanticism — the manifesto and the experiment simultaneously.
German philosophy — the transformative influence
The winter Coleridge spent in Germany (1798–99) studying the language and thought transformed his intellectual life. He read Kant (Critique of Pure Reason — the distinction between Reason and Understanding), Schelling (System des transcendentalen Idealismus — the imagination as absolute), and A.W. Schlegel (Vorlesungen über dramatische Kunst und Literatur — organic form, Shakespeare). These ideas gave him the philosophical framework for the Biographia and the Shakespeare lectures. He was the primary conduit through which German Idealist philosophy entered English Romantic thought.
The French Revolution & political evolution
Coleridge and Southey's Pantisocracy (1794) was directly inspired by the French Revolution's ideals. Coleridge lectured in Bristol in favour of abolition and against the slave trade. By 1798, France: An Ode marks his disillusionment with French imperialism (the Swiss invasion). By 1809–10 (The Friend), he is an explicit conservative — defending the constitution, the Church, and hereditary institutions. By 1816–17 (Biographia, Statesman's Manual), he is the intellectual founder of English Tory Romanticism. This trajectory — from radical to conservative — is shared with Wordsworth and Southey, and has been a major subject of political criticism (McGann, Chandler).
Influences on Coleridge
Milton: The primary poetic influence — Paradise Lost structures Coleridge's understanding of the sublime and of imaginative language.
Shakespeare: His model of organic form and dramatic character — Hamlet as projection of the contemplative temperament.
Kant & Schelling: The philosophical foundations of the imagination/fancy distinction and the primary/secondary imagination.
A.W. Schlegel: Organic form; the rehabilitation of Shakespeare from neoclassical attack.
Hartley: Associationist psychology — which Coleridge embraced in the 1790s (naming his son Hartley) and then systematically dismantled in the Biographia.
Wordsworth: The most complex influence — Coleridge both shaped Wordsworth's ideas and was shaped by them; his best poems and worst failures are both partly caused by Wordsworth.
X

Key Critical Terms

Primary Imagination
The unconscious, universal creative faculty through which all human beings perceive — "the living Power and prime Agent of all human Perception." A finite echo of the infinite divine act of creation. Every person has it; it is not uniquely poetic. Biographia Literaria, Ch. XIII.
Secondary Imagination
The conscious, voluntary creative faculty of the poet — an echo of the primary imagination, "co-existing with the conscious will." It "dissolves, diffuses, dissipates, in order to recreate." It is "essentially vital" — it gives life to fixed and dead objects. Biographia Literaria, Ch. XIII.
Fancy
A lesser mental faculty — "a mode of Memory emancipated from the order of time and space." Fancy takes existing materials and recombines them by association; it does not transform. Clever similes and conceits are Fancy; metaphors that reveal hidden truth are Imagination. Biographia Literaria, Ch. XIII.
Willing suspension of disbelief
The reader's voluntary setting-aside of disbelief in order to receive the emotional effects of supernatural or improbable fiction — "that willing suspension of disbelief for the moment, which constitutes poetic faith." Coined in Biographia Literaria, Ch. XIV. Now applied far beyond its original context.
Organic form
Form that grows from within — inseparable from content, arising from the work's own internal necessity. Opposed to "mechanical form" (imposed from outside). Coleridge used it to rehabilitate Shakespeare's apparent rule-breaking: each play grows its own form. Derived from A.W. Schlegel.
Conversation poem
A critical category coined by George McLean Harper (1928) for Coleridge's blank-verse meditative lyrics: The Eolian Harp, This Lime-Tree Bower, Frost at Midnight, Fears in Solitude, Dejection: An Ode. They share: domestic setting, outward movement into nature, inward return with new understanding.
Marginal gloss
The prose commentary added alongside the verse of The Ancient Mariner in the 1817 Sibylline Leaves edition. Written in a different, cooler register than the poem. Sometimes diverges from the poem's events — creating a dual text. A formal innovation without precedent in English poetry.
Person from Porlock
The unnamed "person on business from Porlock" who interrupted Coleridge's transcription of Kubla Khan, causing the vision to disperse. In Coleridge's 1816 preface. Has become a cultural shorthand for creative interruption. Whether the story is literally true is debated by scholars.
Pantisocracy
The utopian commune planned by Coleridge and Southey (1794) — twelve men and twelve women would settle on the Susquehanna River in Pennsylvania and establish an egalitarian, communal society without property or hierarchy. It never happened; it shaped the early political poems and Coleridge's first marriage.
Accentual verse
Coleridge's metrical innovation in Christabel — each line has four accented syllables but the number of unaccented syllables varies freely. This gave the poem its flexible, speech-like rhythm. Scott adopted it in The Lay of the Last Minstrel (1805), Byron in The Siege of Corinth (1816) — both before Christabel was published.
Biographia Literaria
Coleridge's 1817 two-volume literary autobiography and criticism — the most important work of critical theory in English Romanticism. Contains the imagination/fancy distinction (Ch. XIII), the willing suspension of disbelief (Ch. XIV), and the critique of Wordsworth (Chs. XVII–XXII). Partly plagiarised from Schelling.

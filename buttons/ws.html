William Shakespeare

1564 — 1616

"All the world's a stage, and all the men and women merely players."

I

Life & Biography

Early life
Born 23 April 1564 in Stratford-upon-Avon, Warwickshire. Son of John Shakespeare (glover, wool dealer, later alderman) and Mary Arden (from minor gentry). Attended the King's New School, Stratford — a free grammar school offering Latin, classical rhetoric, Ovid, Virgil, and Cicero. No university education — a fact used against him by the "anti-Stratfordians" and defended vigorously by scholars ever since.
Marriage & family
Married Anne Hathaway in November 1582. She was 26; he was 18. A marriage licence bond was issued in unusual haste — Anne was three months pregnant. They had three children: Susanna (1583), and twins Hamnet and Judith (1585). Hamnet died aged 11 in 1596. Shakespeare's relationship with Anne is much speculated upon — he lived predominantly in London for most of his adult life, returning to Stratford only later. His will famously left Anne the "second-best bed."
The "Lost Years" (1585–1592)
Nothing is documented about Shakespeare between the birth of his twins (1585) and his first mention as a playwright in London (1592). Theories include: serving as a country schoolmaster, working as a butcher's apprentice, joining a travelling theatre company, or going to Lancashire as a player. Robert Greene's 1592 pamphlet attacking him as "an upstart crow" is the first firm evidence of his London theatrical career.
1592
London establishment
Robert Greene's Groats-worth of Wit attacks him — proof he was already successful enough to be resented. Early plays Henry VI (Parts 1–3) and Titus Andronicus performed.
1593–94
Plague, poems & patronage
London theatres closed due to plague. Shakespeare writes Venus and Adonis and The Rape of Lucrece, dedicated to Henry Wriothesley, Earl of Southampton — his primary patron.
1594
Lord Chamberlain's Men
Becomes a founding shareholder and principal playwright for the Lord Chamberlain's Men, the most successful theatre company in London. Richard Burbage was the leading actor; Will Kempe the clown.
1599
The Globe Theatre
The company dismantles The Theatre (Shoreditch) and rebuilds it on Bankside as the Globe. Shakespeare holds a 12.5% share. The Globe holds ~3,000 spectators — groundlings standing, galleries seated. Opens with Julius Caesar.
1603
King's Men
On the accession of James I, the Lord Chamberlain's Men become the King's Men — direct royal patronage. Shakespeare is now a Groom of the Chamber. The company also plays at Blackfriars (indoor, candlelit) from 1608.
1609
Sonnets published
Thomas Thorpe publishes the Sonnets (154 poems) without Shakespeare's apparent authorisation. Dedicated to the mysterious "Mr. W.H." — still debated. The sequence addresses a "Fair Youth," a "Dark Lady," and a "Rival Poet."
1613
Retirement to Stratford
Globe Theatre burns down during a performance of Henry VIII (a cannon misfires). Shakespeare retires to New Place, Stratford. His last sole-authored play is The Tempest (1611); final works are collaborations with John Fletcher.
1616
Death
Dies 23 April 1616 — his 52nd birthday — in Stratford. Cause unknown. Buried in the chancel of Holy Trinity Church. His monument and grave are there still. The Folio is published posthumously in 1623 by John Heminges and Henry Condell.
NET trap: The First Folio (1623) contains 36 plays. Pericles is absent from the First Folio but included in the Third Folio (1664). The question "how many plays did Shakespeare write?" commonly trips students — the standard answer is 37 (or 38–39 if collaborations like The Two Noble Kinsmen and Cardenio are included).
II

Works — Overview

The canon at a glance
37 plays (First Folio + Pericles) in four genres: Tragedies (11), Comedies (17), Histories (10), Romances/Late Plays (4 within Folio). Plus 154 Sonnets, two narrative poems (Venus and Adonis; The Rape of Lucrece), and some disputed shorter poems.

Published seven years after Shakespeare's death by fellow actors John Heminges and Henry Condell. Without it, at least 18 plays would have been lost forever (including Macbeth, Twelfth Night, and The Tempest). The Folio divides the plays into Comedies, Histories, and Tragedies — the three-part division used by most editions since. Originally cost 15 shillings (roughly a week's wages for a skilled craftsman). About 750 copies were printed; ~235 survive. A copy sold at Christie's in 2001 for $6.1 million.

NET fact: The Folio text vs the Quarto texts is an important editorial/bibliographic issue. For Hamlet, there are three versions: Q1 (1603, the "Bad Quarto"), Q2 (1604–5, the "Good Quarto"), and the First Folio text. All three differ significantly.
154 sonnets published 1609. The Shakespearean (or Elizabethan) sonnet: three quatrains (ABAB CDCD EFEF) + a couplet (GG). The couplet typically turns or resolves.

Three addressees:
• Sonnets 1–126: The "Fair Youth" — a young man of beauty and status, urged to procreate (sonnets 1–17) and beloved deeply
• Sonnets 127–152: The "Dark Lady" — dark-complexioned, sexually compelling, morally troubling
• Sonnets 153–154: Conventional love-poetry codas

The "Rival Poet" (Sonnets 78–86) is another figure — possibly Christopher Marlowe, George Chapman, or another.

"Mr. W.H.": The dedicatee is "the onlie begetter." Candidates include Henry Wriothesley (Earl of Southampton), William Herbert (Earl of Pembroke), or even the publisher's friend William Hall.

Famous individual sonnets: 18 ("Shall I compare thee"), 29 ("When in disgrace"), 73 ("That time of year"), 116 ("Let me not to the marriage of true minds"), 130 ("My mistress' eyes").

The Tragedies

Hamlet NET HIGH
c. 1600–01 · Tragedy · Denmark
▼
Tragedy
Plot summary
Prince Hamlet of Denmark is visited by his father's ghost, who reveals he was murdered by Hamlet's uncle Claudius, now king and married to Hamlet's mother Gertrude. Hamlet's tortured delay in taking revenge drives the action. He stages a play-within-a-play to confirm Claudius's guilt; accidentally kills Polonius; drives Ophelia to madness and drowning; is sent to England; returns to a Denmark in crisis. Final duel scene: Gertrude drinks poisoned wine meant for Hamlet; Laertes wounds Hamlet with a poisoned sword; Hamlet kills Claudius; all four die. Horatio survives to tell the tale.
Key characters
• Hamlet: The "melancholy prince" — contemplative, self-doubting, theatrical
• Claudius: The corrupt usurper-king, pragmatic and guilty
• Gertrude: The ambiguous queen — complicit or innocent?
• Ophelia: Destroyed by patriarchal control and Hamlet's cruelty
• Polonius: Verbose counsellor, killed by accident
• Horatio: The rational, loyal friend; the survivor-narrator
Critical themes
• Delay & procrastination: The central Hamlet "problem" — Coleridge's "too much thinking"; Bradley's paralysis of will; Freudian reading (Ernest Jones): Hamlet cannot kill Claudius because he has himself wished to do what Claudius did (Oedipal desire)
• Appearance vs reality: "Seems, madam? Nay, it is. I know not 'seems'"
• Mortality & the absurd: "To be or not to be" — the most famous speech in English literature; "To die, to sleep"
• Madness: Real (Ophelia) vs performed (Hamlet's "antic disposition")
• Revenge tragedy conventions: Ghost, delay, play-within-play, multiple deaths — compare Kyd's The Spanish Tragedy
Key quotations

"To be or not to be, that is the question: / Whether 'tis nobler in the mind to suffer / The slings and arrows of outrageous fortune, / Or to take arms against a sea of troubles, / And by opposing end them."

— Hamlet, III.i

"The lady doth protest too much, methinks."

— Gertrude, III.ii (often misquoted as "the lady protests too much")
Textual issues
Three texts: Q1 (1603) — "Bad Quarto," possibly from memory; Q2 (1604–5) — closest to Shakespeare's manuscript; Folio (1623) — later revision. Most editions conflate Q2 and Folio. A major NET topic.
Film adaptations
• Laurence Olivier (1948) — Won Best Picture Oscar. Black-and-white, expressionist, Freudian. The definitive classical version.
• Grigori Kozintsev (1964, USSR) — Pasternak translation. Often cited as the greatest Hamlet film overall. Deeply political.
• Franco Zeffirelli (1990) — Mel Gibson as Hamlet. Accessible, action-oriented.
• Kenneth Branagh (1996) — Full uncut text (~4 hrs). Derek Jacobi as Claudius; Kate Winslet as Ophelia. An imperial adaptation.
• Michael Almereyda (2000) — Modern New York setting. Ethan Hawke as a film student. Interesting but divisive.
• Benedict Cumberbatch (2015, NT Live) — Stage → screen. Controversial staging; huge commercial success.
Othello NET HIGH
c. 1603–04 · Tragedy · Venice / Cyprus
▼
Tragedy
Plot summary
Othello, a Moorish general in Venetian service, secretly marries Desdemona. His ensign Iago, passed over for promotion, engineers Othello's destruction by making him believe Desdemona has been unfaithful with Cassio. The handkerchief (Othello's first gift to Desdemona) is planted as "proof." Othello smothers Desdemona. Emilia (Iago's wife) exposes the deception; Othello kills himself; Iago is captured.
Critical themes
• Race & otherness: Othello's race is central — the play stages and interrogates the racism of Venetian society and Othello's own internalised vulnerability
• Jealousy ("the green-eyed monster"): Both Iago's motiveless malignity and Othello's catastrophic jealousy
• Iago's villainy: Coleridge's "motiveless malignity" — Iago's evil seems to exceed his stated reasons. He is one of the great psychological puzzles in literature
• Honour & reputation: "Who steals my purse steals trash... But he that filches from me my good name..."
• Postcolonial reading: Othello as a figure shaped and destroyed by colonial/imperial power structures (Loomba, Spivak)
Film adaptations
• Orson Welles (1952) — Shot over three years due to funding crises. Brilliant expressionist cinematography. Won Palme d'Or at Cannes.
• Laurence Olivier (1965) — Olivier in controversial blackface. Now studied as a document of its era's racial assumptions.
• Stuart Burge (1965) — Olivier's stage performance filmed.
• Oliver Parker (1995) — Laurence Fishburne (first Black actor to play Othello in a major film); Kenneth Branagh as Iago. Accessible and well-acted.
• O (2001) — Modern high school adaptation. Mekhi Phifer, Julia Stiles. Interesting transposition.
• National Theatre Live (2013) — Adrian Lester and Rory Kinnear. Widely praised stage-to-screen.
King Lear NET HIGH
c. 1605–06 · Tragedy · Britain
▼
Tragedy
Plot summary
Aged king Lear divides his kingdom among his three daughters based on declarations of love. Goneril and Regan flatter him; Cordelia refuses to speak falsely and is disinherited. Lear is progressively stripped of his knights and his sanity by his two older daughters. He wanders on a storm-wracked heath with the Fool, Edgar (disguised as "Poor Tom"), and Kent. A parallel subplot: Gloucester is deceived by his bastard son Edmund into blinding and exile. The play ends in catastrophe — Cordelia hanged, Lear dying over her body. Edmund, Goneril, and Regan all die. Edgar survives.
Critical themes
• Nothing: "Nothing will come of nothing" — the word "nothing" appears 34 times
• Madness & vision: Lear's madness paradoxically gives him insight he lacked as a king
• Nature: Edmund's "nature" (amoral, Hobbesian) vs the play's invocation of nature as moral order
• Suffering & theodicy: A.C. Bradley called it the greatest of the tragedies for its vision of unrelieved suffering
• The two texts problem: Q1 (1608) and Folio versions differ significantly — the Folio cuts the Fool's final scene and adds a different ending. Some scholars argue they are two distinct plays
Famous critical responses
• Samuel Johnson found the death of Cordelia too painful to reread
• Nahum Tate (1681) rewrote the play with a happy ending — Cordelia survives and marries Edgar — which was the standard stage version until 1838
• Jan Kott's Shakespeare Our Contemporary (1964): Lear as Beckett-esque absurdism — the great heath becomes the endgame stage
• Peter Brook's 1971 film directly inspired by Kott: bleak, existentialist
Film adaptations
• Peter Brook (1971) — Paul Scofield. Stark, desolate, Beckett-influenced. Essential viewing.
• Grigori Kozintsev (1970, USSR) — Another masterpiece from the director of Hamlet. Humanist reading.
• Ran (Akira Kurosawa, 1985) — Japanese transposition. An aging warlord divides his kingdom among three sons. Visually extraordinary.
• Richard Eyre (1998, BBC) — Ian Holm as Lear. Intimate and psychologically devastating.
• Anthony Hopkins (2018, Amazon) — Hopkins and Emma Thompson. Technically competent but divisive.
Macbeth NET HIGH
c. 1606 · Tragedy · Scotland
▼
Tragedy
Plot summary
Scottish general Macbeth hears a prophecy from three witches that he will become king. Encouraged by his wife, he murders King Duncan and seizes the throne. But guilt, paranoia, and further murders (Banquo, Macduff's family) compound. Lady Macbeth sleepwalks and is tormented by guilt ("Out, damned spot!"). Macbeth, seemingly protected by further prophecies, is killed by Macduff (born by caesarean — "not of woman born"). Malcolm is restored.
Critical themes
• Ambition: "Vaulting ambition, which o'erleaps itself" — the play is the most concentrated study of ambition in English literature
• The supernatural: Witches, daggers, ghosts — the supernatural externalises internal psychological states
• Gender & power: Lady Macbeth's "unsex me here" speech — she inverts gender norms to claim masculine murderous agency. Later she collapses
• Time & prophecy: The equivocating witches — "fair is foul and foul is fair"
• Political legitimacy: Written for James I (who believed in witchcraft and traced his lineage to Banquo). A topical play about usurpation and divine right.
The "Scottish Play" superstition
Theatre tradition holds Macbeth is cursed — actors say "the Scottish play" rather than the name backstage. Possible origins: the play's dark material; early accidents in productions; the genuine Witches' invocations.
Film adaptations
• Orson Welles (1948) — Low-budget but atmospheric. Shot in 23 days.
• Akira Kurosawa — Throne of Blood (1957): Masterpiece. Feudal Japan, Noh theatre aesthetics. Often called the greatest Shakespeare adaptation.
• Roman Polanski (1971) — Made after the Manson murders. Graphically violent, psychologically raw.
• Geoffrey Wright (2006) — Australia, modern gangland setting. Atmospheric.
• Justin Kurzel (2015) — Michael Fassbender, Marion Cotillard. Visually stunning Scottish Highlands; emotionally cool.
• Joel Coen (The Tragedy of Macbeth, 2021) — Denzel Washington, Frances McDormand. Black-and-white, expressionist, theatre-on-film. Remarkable.
Romeo and Juliet
c. 1594–96 · Tragedy · Verona
▼
Tragedy
Critical significance
Unusual among Shakespeare's tragedies in that the protagonists are not undone by a tragic flaw (hamartia) but by circumstance — the feud, timing, misunderstanding. Sometimes called a "fate tragedy." The play transforms the earlier sonnet form — Romeo and Juliet exchange a sonnet at their first meeting (Act I, Sc.5). Key source: Arthur Brooke's narrative poem The Tragicall Historye of Romeus and Juliet (1562).
Film adaptations
• George Cukor (1936) — Norma Shearer and Leslie Howard. Classical Hollywood.
• Renato Castellani (1954) — Shot on location in Verona.
• Franco Zeffirelli (1968) — Definitive traditional version. Young actors (Olivia Hussey, Leonard Whiting). Won Oscars for Cinematography and Costume Design.
• Baz Luhrmann (1996) — William Shakespeare's Romeo + Juliet. Modern Verona Beach, guns instead of swords, DiCaprio and Danes. Postmodern, kinetic — divided opinion but hugely influential.
• Carlo Carlei (2013) — Conventional but unremarkable.
• Tom & Gerry (2021 Hamlet-adjacent) — Various NT Live recordings.
Antony and Cleopatra
c. 1606–07 · Tragedy · Rome / Egypt
▼
Tragedy
Critical significance
Often considered Shakespeare's most technically ambitious play: 42 scenes, shifts between Rome and Egypt, dozens of characters. The conflict between Roman duty (Antony's military and political identity) and Egyptian pleasure (Cleopatra's world) drives the tragedy. A.C. Bradley excluded it from his "four great tragedies" — later critics elevated it. Cleopatra is arguably Shakespeare's most complex female role.
Key lines

"Age cannot wither her, nor custom stale / Her infinite variety."

— Enobarbus on Cleopatra, II.ii
Film adaptations
• Joseph L. Mankiewicz (1963) — Elizabeth Taylor and Richard Burton. Famous for its monumental budget ($44m, nearly bankrupted Fox) and the real-life affair between the leads.
• BBC Shakespeare (1981) — Complete BBC series version.
• Various National Theatre Live versions.
Julius Caesar
c. 1599 · Tragedy · Rome
▼
Tragedy
Critical significance
Despite the title, Brutus is the tragic protagonist — his idealism and fatal misjudgment (allowing Antony to speak at Caesar's funeral) bring catastrophe. Mark Antony's funeral oration ("Friends, Romans, countrymen") is a masterclass in rhetoric and manipulation. The play raises questions about tyrannicide, political idealism, and the relationship between private morality and public action.
Film adaptations
• Joseph L. Mankiewicz (1953) — Marlon Brando as Antony, John Gielgud as Cassius. Brando's performance widely praised as proof Method acting worked for Shakespeare.
• Stuart Burge (1970) — Charlton Heston, John Gielgud (now as Caesar).
• Nicholas Hytner (2018, NT Live) — Modern political setting. David Morrissey as Caesar, Ben Whishaw as Brutus.
IV

The Comedies

Shakespeare's comedy — conventions
Shakespearean comedy typically features: a movement from "normal" world to green/forest/alternative world and back; mistaken identity (often through disguise, frequently female characters cross-dressing); multiple romantic plots converging in marriage at the end; a blocking figure (jealous parent, villain, misanthrope) who is overcome; festive release and reconciliation. Northrop Frye's "green world" theory is essential here.
A Midsummer Night's Dream NET HIGH
c. 1595–96 · Comedy · Athens / The Forest
▼
Comedy
Structure
Three interlocking plots: the Athenian lovers (Hermia, Lysander, Helena, Demetrius) fleeing parental authority; the fairy world (Oberon, Titania, Puck) whose quarrel and magic disorder the human world; the "Mechanicals" (Bottom, Quince et al.) rehearsing a play-within-the-play — Pyramus and Thisbe — for Theseus's wedding.
Critical themes
• Dream, illusion, theatre: The play interrogates the nature of imagination and reality — "The lunatic, the lover, and the poet / Are of imagination all compact"
• Love's irrationality: The lovers' changeable affections under the influence of the love-juice
• Gender & hierarchy: Titania's subjection to Oberon; Hermia's defiance of her father and the law of Athens
• Class & performance: The Mechanicals' earnest amateur theatricals — a comic meditation on theatrical representation itself
Film adaptations
• Max Reinhardt & William Dieterle (1935) — James Cagney as Bottom. Spectacular Hollywood fairy-tale production.
• Peter Hall (1968) — Judi Dench as Titania. Shot in muddy woods — deliberately anti-romantic.
• Michael Hoffman (1999) — Michelle Pfeiffer, Kevin Kline, Rupert Everett. Lush 19th-century Italy setting.
• The Dream (various NT Live, RSC recordings) — Many acclaimed stage versions.
The Merchant of Venice Trap!
c. 1596–97 · Comedy / Problem Play · Venice / Belmont
▼
ComedyProblem
The "problem" issue
Classified as a comedy in the First Folio but deeply uncomfortable. Shylock's demand for a "pound of flesh" is defeated by Portia's legal quibble; he is forced to convert to Christianity — a "happy" ending for Elizabethan audiences, a horrifying one for modern readers. Debates: is the play anti-Semitic, or is it critiquing anti-Semitism? Shylock's "Hath not a Jew eyes?" speech gives him a humanity that destabilises the comic resolution.
Critical themes
• Mercy vs justice: Portia's "The quality of mercy is not strained" — but mercy is not extended to Shylock
• Commerce & bonds: The entire play is structured around financial obligation
• Gender & the law: Portia operates in the male world only through disguise
Film adaptations
• Michael Radford (2004) — Al Pacino as Shylock. Jeremy Irons, Joseph Fiennes. The most serious modern film version.
• BBC Television (1980) — Warren Mitchell as Shylock.
• Various National Theatre productions — Jonathan Pryce (1999), Patrick Stewart (2003) highly regarded.
Twelfth Night NET HIGH
c. 1601–02 · Comedy · Illyria
▼
Comedy
Plot & themes
Viola, shipwrecked in Illyria, disguises herself as a boy (Cesario) and serves Duke Orsino, who is in love with Countess Olivia. Olivia falls in love with Cesario (Viola); Orsino falls in love with Viola. Viola's twin Sebastian arrives; Olivia marries Sebastian; Orsino accepts Viola. Subplot: Malvolio, Olivia's steward, is humiliated by a practical joke. The play questions: what is the nature of love? of identity? of gender?
Critical note
Malvolio's punishment — often played for comedy — has attracted increasingly sympathetic rereadings. He is a figure of Protestant earnestness mocked by aristocratic excess. Some productions make his exit genuinely dark. "I'll be revenged on the whole pack of you" — does the comedy fully absorb this?
Film adaptations
• Trevor Nunn (1996) — Helena Bonham Carter, Imogen Stubbs, Ben Kingsley as Feste. Rural 19th-century setting. Melancholic and beautiful.
• Andy Fickman (She's the Man, 2006) — Loose teen adaptation. Amanda Bynes.
• Emma Rice (Globe, 2017) — Controversial modern staging; NT Live available.
Much Ado About Nothing
c. 1598–99 · Comedy · Messina, Sicily
▼
Comedy
Critical significance
Two plots: Benedick and Beatrice (the "merry war" couple, tricked into admitting love by their friends) and Claudio and Hero (romantic love shattered by slander, restored by apparent death and resurrection). The Beatrice-Benedick subplot, not the main plot, is what endures. Dogberry and the Watch provide brilliant malapropism comedy.
Film adaptations
• Kenneth Branagh (1993) — Branagh and Emma Thompson as Benedick and Beatrice. Sunny Tuscany. The most popular Shakespeare comedy on film — warm, funny, masterfully performed.
• Joss Whedon (2012) — Shot in black-and-white in Whedon's own home over 12 days. Amy Acker and Alexis Denisof. Surprisingly effective modern take.
As You Like It
c. 1599–1600 · Comedy · Court / Arden Forest
▼
Comedy
Critical significance
The Forest of Arden as green world; Rosalind (disguised as Ganymede) as one of Shakespeare's greatest heroines. Jaques's "Seven Ages of Man" speech ("All the world's a stage") is here. Touchstone as a sophisticated fool. The play is lighter and more self-aware than many comedies — it "likes it" itself, bracketed by the meta-theatrical. Source: Thomas Lodge's Rosalynde (1590).
Film adaptations
• Paul Czinner (1936) — Laurence Olivier as Orlando.
• Christine Edzard (1992) — Set in modern London with homeless people as the Forest exiles. Provocative.
• Kenneth Branagh (2006) — Set in Japan. Bryce Dallas Howard, David Oyelowo. Unusual but interesting.
Problem plays Important
F.S. Boas coined the term "problem plays" in 1896 for Hamlet, Troilus and Cressida, All's Well That Ends Well, and Measure for Measure — plays that resist comfortable generic classification. They end in marriage but without comic resolution; they raise moral questions the plot cannot answer.

Measure for Measure: Isabella faces Angelo's sexual blackmail (her brother's life for her virginity). One of Shakespeare's darkest works — the "bed trick," the "head trick," the "problem" of justice without mercy.

Troilus and Cressida: A sardonic retelling of the Trojan War. Neither tragedy nor comedy — all heroism is deflated, all love betrayed. Thersites's misanthropy dominates. Often called the most "modern" Shakespeare play.
V

The History Plays

The two tetralogies
Shakespeare's 10 history plays divide into two groups covering English history from 1398 to 1485:

First Tetralogy (written first, covering later events): Henry VI Parts 1–3, Richard III
Second Tetralogy (written later, covering earlier events): Richard II, Henry IV Parts 1–2, Henry V

Together they constitute a vast "epic" of the Wars of the Roses — from Richard II's deposition through to the Tudor settlement. The Second Tetralogy is generally considered the greater achievement.
Richard II
c. 1595 · History · Medieval England
▼
History
Critical significance
The deposition of Richard II — a figure of language, ceremony, and aesthetic sensibility — by the practical, political Bolingbroke (Henry IV). The play is structured as a fall from kingly poetry to political prose. Richard's "deposition scene" (IV.i) is a theatrical masterpiece of self-dramatisation. The Essex Rebellion of 1601 commissioned a special performance of this play — its deposition scene was politically explosive.
Film adaptations
• Derek Jacobi (BBC, 1978) — The definitive screen version.
• The Hollow Crown (BBC, 2012) — Ben Whishaw as Richard II. Part of a landmark BBC series covering the complete Second Tetralogy. Whishaw's performance is widely praised as the best screen Richard.
• Richard Eyre (2023) — Simon Russell Beale. NT Live broadcast.
Henry IV Parts 1 & 2 + Falstaff
c. 1596–98 · History
▼
History
Falstaff
Sir John Falstaff is one of the greatest comic creations in world literature — a fat, cowardly, witty, life-loving knight whose tavern world (Eastcheap, the Boar's Head) offers an anarchic alternative to the world of political history. His relationship with Prince Hal is the emotional core of both plays. The rejection of Falstaff at the end of Part 2 ("I know thee not, old man. Fall to thy prayers") is one of the most moving moments in Shakespeare — and one of the most debated. Does Hal betray Falstaff, or must he?
Film adaptations
• Orson Welles — Chimes at Midnight (1965): A composite of the Henry IV plays and Henry V, centred on Falstaff (played by Welles himself). Often considered Welles's greatest film and one of the greatest Shakespeare films ever made.
• The Hollow Crown (BBC, 2012) — Jeremy Irons as Henry IV, Simon Russell Beale as Falstaff. Exceptional.
• The King (Netflix, 2019) — Timothée Chalamet as Hal/Henry V. Joel Edgerton as Falstaff. Loose, revisionist adaptation.
Henry V
c. 1598–99 · History · England / France
▼
History
The St Crispin's Day speech

"We few, we happy few, we band of brothers; / For he to-day that sheds his blood with me / Shall be my brother..."

— Henry V, IV.iii
Critical ambivalence
The play can be read as a celebration of English nationhood and heroic kingship — or as an ironic, even critical, deconstruction of war propaganda. The Chorus's constant apologies for the stage's inadequacy draw attention to theatrical illusion. Henry's order to kill the French prisoners, and his awkward wooing of Katherine, complicate heroic readings. Tillyard's "Elizabethan World Picture" reading vs. New Historicist readings (Greenblatt) are key critical positions.
Film adaptations
• Laurence Olivier (1944) — Made as wartime propaganda during WWII. Opens on the Globe Theatre, moves to idealised Technicolor France. Essential viewing for understanding Shakespeare-as-ideology.
• Kenneth Branagh (1989) — The post-Falklands version. Grittier, more ambivalent, rain-soaked. Equals Olivier in many critics' view.
• The Hollow Crown (BBC, 2012) — Tom Hiddleston as Henry. Acclaimed.
Richard III NET HIGH
c. 1592–93 · History / Tragedy · England
▼
History
Critical significance
Opens with the most electrifying villain's soliloquy in English drama: "Now is the winter of our discontent." Richard III is a Vice figure from the medieval morality tradition — he directly addresses the audience, makes us complicit in his scheming. He is also a commentary on Tudor historiography: Shakespeare's source is Thomas More's History of Richard III and Holinshed's Chronicles — both Tudor propaganda. The historical Richard may have been far less monstrous.
Film adaptations
• Laurence Olivier (1955) — The definitive version for generations. Olivier's Richard is a theatrical tour de force.
• Ian McKellen (Richard Loncraine, 1995) — 1930s fascist Britain setting. McKellen and Annette Bening. Startlingly relevant transposition. Highly recommended.
• Al Pacino — Looking for Richard (1996): Documentary/adaptation hybrid. Pacino explores the play with American actors. Fascinating.
• The Hollow Crown: Wars of the Roses (BBC, 2016) — Benedict Cumberbatch as Richard III. Polarising performance; grandly staged.
VI

The Late Romances

What are the romances?
The final phase of Shakespeare's career (c.1608–13). Not named "romances" in the First Folio — the term is a later critical construction. Characteristics: magical or improbable events; long stretches of time and far journeys; separation of families; near-tragedy averted; reconciliation and forgiveness at the end; redemption through the younger generation; theme of loss and restoration. Sometimes called "tragicomedies" following Guarini's theoretical genre.
The Tempest NET HIGH
c. 1610–11 · Romance · An Island
▼
Romance
Plot summary
Prospero, the rightful Duke of Milan, has been exiled to an island for 12 years with his daughter Miranda. He controls the island's spirit Ariel and the "savage" native Caliban. When his usurping brother Antonio passes by sea, Prospero raises a tempest and brings his enemies to the island — where he stages a drama of punishment, repentance, and reconciliation. Ferdinand (the King of Naples' son) falls in love with Miranda. Prospero forgives his enemies, relinquishes his magic, and will return to Milan.
Critical themes
• Colonialism: The dominant modern reading — Prospero as coloniser, Caliban as the dispossessed native. Caliban says: "This island's mine, by Sycorax my mother, / Which thou tak'st from me." Caribbean postcolonial writers (Aimé Césaire's Une Tempête, 1969; George Lamming, Roberto Fernández Retamar) have rewritten the play from Caliban's perspective
• Art vs nature: Prospero's magic as theatrical art; the tension between civilisation and instinct
• Forgiveness & power: Does Prospero genuinely forgive, or merely reassert dominance?
• The authorial farewell?: Prospero's "Epilogue" — "Now my charms are all o'erthrown / And what strength I have's mine own" — is traditionally read as Shakespeare's farewell to the stage. This is a critical convention, not a biographical fact
• Language & power: Caliban's famous line — "You taught me language, and my profit on't / Is I know how to curse"
Film adaptations
• Derek Jarman (1979) — Camp, surrealist, punk-inflected. Heathcote Williams as Prospero. Cult classic.
• Paul Mazursky (Tempest, 1982) — Loose modern retelling. John Cassavetes.
• Peter Greenaway (Prospero's Books, 1991) — John Gielgud as Prospero; every word of the text spoken by Prospero. Visually spectacular, intellectually demanding.
• Julie Taymor (2010) — Helen Mirren as Prospera (gender-swapped). Russell Brand as Trinculo. Interesting but uneven.
• Sam Mendes (2021, Donmar/NT) — Stage version with multiple national productions available online.
The Winter's Tale
c. 1609–11 · Romance · Sicilia / Bohemia
▼
Romance
Critical significance
Leontes's sudden, unexplained jealousy (one of the most psychologically radical moments in Shakespeare — no Iago needed) destroys his family. His wife Hermione apparently dies; his infant daughter Perdita is abandoned on the Bohemian coast. Sixteen years pass (announced by Time as Chorus). Perdita is found; Hermione is revealed alive as a "statue" that comes to life. The play moves from tragedy to pastoral comedy to a miraculous, if fragile, restoration. The statue scene is one of the most beautiful moments in the entire canon.
Film adaptations
• BBC Television (1981) — Jane Lapotaire as Hermione.
• The Donmar Warehouse (2015, NT Live) — Kenneth Branagh and Judi Dench.
• Gareth Hyatt (Sheffield Crucible, 2022) — Recently broadcast; highly praised.

Film Adaptations — Essential Guide

Why film matters for NET
NET Paper 2 increasingly includes questions on stage history and adaptation. Understanding how films interpret, transpose, and transform Shakespeare tests your grasp of themes and critical frameworks. Directors named below are frequently tested.

The five essential directors

Laurence Olivier (1907–1989)
Henry V (1944) · Hamlet (1948) · Richard III (1955) · Othello (1965)
The actor-director who brought Shakespeare to mainstream cinema. His Henry V (1944) was commissioned as WWII propaganda by the British government — and is a study in how Shakespeare can be mobilised for national ideology. Hamlet (1948) won four Oscars including Best Picture — the only Shakespeare film to do so. His approach was theatrical, classical, and shaped by his stage experience at the Old Vic.
★★★★★
Orson Welles (1915–1985)
Macbeth (1948) · Othello (1952) · Chimes at Midnight (1965)
The most cinematically inventive Shakespeare director. Chimes at Midnight — a composite of the Henry IV plays and Henry V, centred on Falstaff (played by Welles) — is considered by many critics the greatest Shakespeare film ever made. Othello (1952) was shot over three years in Morocco as funding ran out; the result is breathtaking expressionist cinema. Welles transformed Shakespeare into cinema rather than filming theatre.
★★★★★
Akira Kurosawa (1910–1998)
Throne of Blood (1957, Macbeth) · Ran (1985, King Lear)
Kurosawa's transpositions into feudal Japan — using Noh theatre aesthetics, samurai culture, and Japanese psychology — are widely regarded as the finest Shakespeare "translations" on film. Throne of Blood transforms Macbeth's supernatural machinery into Noh theatrical convention; the final scene (arrow-pierced Washizu) is among the most visceral in cinema history. Ran (1985) — King Lear with three sons — won the Academy Award for Costume Design. Essential viewing for postcolonial and cross-cultural approaches.
★★★★★
Kenneth Branagh (b. 1960)
Henry V (1989) · Much Ado (1993) · Othello (1995) · Hamlet (1996) · Love's Labour's Lost (2000) · As You Like It (2006) · Cinderella (2015) · Macbeth (2021, Belfast)
The most prolific Shakespeare filmmaker since Olivier — whom he consciously emulates and contests. His Henry V (1989) is explicitly post-Falklands and anti-heroic compared to Olivier's wartime version. His Hamlet (1996) is the only full, uncut text on film (4 hours) — shot in a Blenheim Palace stand-in, with an all-star cast. His Much Ado About Nothing (1993) remains the most commercially successful Shakespeare comedy film. He has democratised Shakespeare for global audiences.
★★★★★
Baz Luhrmann (b. 1962)
Romeo + Juliet (1996)
Romeo + Juliet (1996) was a cultural earthquake — Shakespeare delivered in MTV aesthetic, with the original language, to teenagers who had never seen him performed. The film uses the text's word "sword" to mean a gun brand; "Mantua" is a city; the setting is chaotic, multicultural "Verona Beach." Luhrmann's maximalism either brilliantly updates the play or traduces it — this debate is itself a useful critical exercise on adaptation theory.
★★★★★

Other significant adaptations

Grigori Kozintsev (USSR)
Hamlet (1964) · King Lear (1970)
Russian adaptations using Boris Pasternak (Hamlet) and Yuri Yarvet (Lear) translations. Shot in harsh black-and-white; politically resonant with Soviet experience of tyranny and oppression. Kozintsev's Hamlet is often named alongside Welles's Chimes at Midnight as the greatest Shakespeare films not in English.
The BBC Shakespeare Series (1978–1985)
All 37 plays · Television
The BBC collaborated with Time-Life to produce all 37 plays. Uneven in quality — some are stiff studio productions, others (Derek Jacobi's Richard II, Jonathan Miller's Othello, Michael Hordern's King Lear) are genuinely excellent. The series is an invaluable resource; many productions are freely available online.
The Hollow Crown (BBC, 2012 & 2016)
Richard II · Henry IV 1&2 · Henry V · Henry VI 1-3 · Richard III
The most ambitious Shakespeare project in British television. Series 1 (Second Tetralogy): Ben Whishaw (Richard II), Jeremy Irons (Henry IV), Tom Hiddleston (Henry V). Series 2 (First Tetralogy, Wars of the Roses): Ben Whishaw, Sophie Okonedo, Benedict Cumberbatch. Cinematic quality, strong performances, and coherent vision of the full historical arc. Essential for anyone studying the history plays.
Shakespeare in Love (1998)
Dir: John Madden · Joseph Fiennes, Gwyneth Paltrow
Won 7 Oscars including Best Picture (controversially over Saving Private Ryan). A fictional account of the creation of Romeo and Juliet. Not an adaptation of a play but a film about Shakespeare. Important for cultural studies; its version of Shakespeare-as-romantic-hero is both charming and historically suspect. Useful to discuss the mythology of Shakespeare's biography.

VIII

Major Themes

Power, legitimacy & usurpation
The most persistent concern across the histories and tragedies. Shakespeare constantly tests the source of political authority: divine right (Richard II), military success (Henry V), charisma and Machiavellian manipulation (Richard III, Iago, Edmund), or the mystical beyond politics (Prospero). The relationship between private virtue and public effectiveness — what Machiavelli called virtù — is tested repeatedly. No play fully endorses divine right; few endorse its alternatives.
Gender, sexuality & disguise
Shakespeare's theatre used only male actors — women's roles were played by boy actors. This creates layers of gender performance and theatrical self-consciousness. In Twelfth Night, a boy actor plays Viola, who disguises as a boy, who is wooed by a woman, who reminds Olivia of her brother — the gender loops are vertiginous. Many comedies feature female characters (Rosalind, Viola, Portia) who are more intelligent, resourceful, and autonomous than the men who nominally control them. The comedies resolve in marriage; the tragedies often destroy the women (Ophelia, Desdemona, Lady Macbeth).
Order & disorder / the body politic
The Elizabethan world picture (E.M.W. Tillyard) posits a divinely ordered cosmos in which hierarchy — God, angels, monarch, nobility, commons, animals — is sacred and any disruption brings chaos. The Ulysses speech in Troilus and Cressida ("Take but degree away, untune that string, / And hark what discord follows") articulates this. Critics have challenged Tillyard: Shakespeare's plays don't simply endorse this order — they test it, question it, and sometimes dramatise its breakdown with evident sympathy.
Appearance vs reality
Theatre is itself an art of appearances — Shakespeare is acutely aware of this. Characters deceive and are deceived; clothing, disguise, and performance conceal identity; reality is unstable. The plays are full of actors playing actors playing characters. The theatrum mundi — world-as-stage — metaphor runs from Jaques's "Seven Ages" to Prospero's "we are such stuff as dreams are made on." The distinction between the "seeming" and the "being" is tested across genres.
Time, mutability & mortality
From the Sonnets ("Shall I compare thee to a summer's day... thy eternal summer shall not fade") to the romances' 16-year gaps, Shakespeare is obsessed with time's destructiveness and art's defiance of it. The great tragedies all turn on timing and urgency. The Histories are haunted by the irreversibility of political decisions. The romances counteract time with miraculous restoration — but fragility remains.
Language, rhetoric & silence
Shakespeare's language is itself a subject of the plays. Characters use different registers — prose for lower-class or comic scenes, verse for elevated moments; the shift between them is always significant. Cordelia's "Nothing" and her silence, Iago's manipulation of rhetoric, Falstaff's verbal exuberance, Hamlet's wordplay — language is the arena where power is exercised and resisted. Some of the most powerful moments in Shakespeare are moments of silence or broken speech.
IX

Critical Approaches to Shakespeare

A.C. Bradley (1904) Character-based criticism. Shakespearean Tragedy analyses Hamlet, Othello, Lear, Macbeth as if characters are real people with psychological depth. Enormously influential; now criticised for ignoring theatrical and textual conditions.
Tillyard — Elizabethan World Picture Shakespeare upholds the divinely ordered hierarchy. Challenged by New Historicists who argue this conservatively misreads the plays.
New Criticism / Close Reading Focus on the text itself — imagery, ambiguity, structure. G. Wilson Knight's The Wheel of Fire — reading plays as unified "spatial" poems.
New Historicism (Greenblatt) Renaissance Self-Fashioning (1980). Shakespeare's plays embedded in the power structures of Elizabethan/Jacobean culture. Text and context inseparable.
Feminist Criticism Lisa Jardine, Coppélia Kahn, Janet Adelman. How gender ideology shapes and is challenged by the plays. Gertrude, Lady Macbeth, and Cleopatra reread as sites of patriarchal anxiety.
Postcolonial The Tempest as colonial allegory (Caliban = colonised subject). Ania Loomba, Jonathan Dollimore. Caribbean and African rewritings (Césaire's Une Tempête).
Psychoanalytic Ernest Jones — Hamlet's Oedipal complex. Janet Adelman — Suffocating Mothers: mothers as sources of male anxiety across the tragedies.
Performance / Theatre Studies Meaning is made in performance, not the text. W.B. Worthen, J.L. Styan. The instability of the "text" when plays are understood as scripts.
Presentism Reading Shakespeare through the lens of present-day concerns — disability, ecology, race, sexuality. Terence Hawkes, Hugh Grady.
Ecocriticism Recent approach. Shakespeare's nature imagery and the green world. Reading the Forest of Arden, the heath in Lear, Prospero's island through environmental frameworks.

Key Critical Terms

Hamartia
The "tragic flaw" — Aristotle's concept from the Poetics. The protagonist's error of judgement or character flaw that triggers their fall. Macbeth's ambition; Othello's jealousy; Lear's pride. Note: Bradley's application of hamartia to Shakespeare is questioned — Aristotle was writing about Greek drama.
Anagnorisis
The moment of recognition or discovery in a tragedy — when the protagonist recognises who they truly are or what has happened. Lear recognising Cordelia; Othello recognising Desdemona's innocence too late.
Catharsis
Aristotle's term for the emotional purgation (of pity and fear) produced by tragedy. Debated: is it purification, clarification, or emotional release? How does this apply to Jacobean tragedy's darker endings?
Soliloquy
A speech delivered by a character alone on stage — the audience is the privileged interlocutor. Gives access to inner thought. Hamlet's "To be or not to be"; Macbeth's "Is this a dagger"; Iago's plotting speeches. Distinct from an aside (brief, overheard) or a monologue (long speech to other characters).
Iambic pentameter
The dominant verse form: 10 syllables, alternating unstressed/stressed (da-DUM da-DUM da-DUM da-DUM da-DUM). "To BE or NOT to BE, that IS the QUES-tion." Variations — feminine endings, trochaic inversions, spondees — carry expressive weight. Blank verse = unrhymed iambic pentameter.
Aside
Brief remark made to the audience that other characters on stage cannot hear. Iago uses asides constantly to maintain our complicit awareness of his scheming while performing innocence to others.
Malapropism
Comic misuse of a similar-sounding word. Dogberry in Much Ado: "Comparisons are odorous" (odious). A device for characterising uneducated characters trying to speak above their register.
Quarto / Folio
Quarto: a smaller, cheaper format — most Shakespeare plays first appeared in quarto during his lifetime. Some are "bad quartos" (pirated, memorially reconstructed), some "good quartos" (authorially sanctioned). The First Folio (1623) is a collected, large-format edition of 36 plays. Textual scholarship involves determining which text is more authoritative for each play.
Vice figure
A character type from medieval morality plays — a scheming, comic-sinister figure who confides in the audience and engineers harm. Richard III, Iago, and Edmund are all heirs to the Vice tradition. Important for understanding how they work theatrically (direct address, self-delighting villainy).
Revenge tragedy
A genre (derived partly from Seneca) characterised by: a ghost demanding revenge; a protagonist who delays; a play-within-a-play or other theatrics; multiple deaths in a final catastrophe. Kyd's The Spanish Tragedy (c.1587) establishes the genre; Hamlet is the apotheosis.
Pastoralism
A literary convention idealising rural/natural life as a retreat from corrupt court/city. As You Like It, The Winter's Tale, and The Tempest all use pastoral frameworks — then complicate or undercut them.
Metatheatre
Drama that is aware of itself as drama — plays that draw attention to their own theatricality. The play-within-a-play in Hamlet; the Mechanicals' rehearsal in A Midsummer Night's Dream; Prospero as stage-manager; the Chorus in Henry V apologising for the stage's inadequacy. A consistent Shakespearean strategy.
